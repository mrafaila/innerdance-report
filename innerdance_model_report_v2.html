HRV Metrics — Choice, Relevance, and Health Impact

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) measures the fluctuation in time between consecutive heartbeats (R-R intervals). A healthy autonomic nervous system produces irregular intervals — this variability is adaptive, not noise. Four metrics were chosen for this study because together they cover the time domain, Poincaré geometry, and nonlinear fractal structure of the heartbeat — and each remains interpretable across the specific constraints of innerdance data (short windows, slow breathing, single-sensor Polar H10).

Metric What it measures Why chosen for this study Clinical evidence Subjective experience Validity caveat
RMSSD
Root Mean Square of Successive Differences · ms		 Beat-to-beat parasympathetic (vagal) tone. Captures how much each heartbeat deviates from the one before it. Gold-standard resting vagal marker; simple to compute; provides pre-session baseline anchor. Used to establish P1's individual reference (104 ms at rest). Higher resting RMSSD associated with lower all-cause mortality (Shaffer & Ginsberg 2017), reduced depression and anxiety risk, better cardiovascular resilience. Athletes typically 60–100 ms; sedentary adults 20–40 ms. When high: chest opens, breathing deepens, a felt sense of ease and safety — the heart has "slack." When low: a subtle tightening, more rigid rhythm — the body is mobilising, not distressed. ⚠ Invalid during innerdance when breathing <9 bpm (McCreary 2022). Use only against resting baseline, not within-session comparisons.
SD2
Poincaré long-axis · ms		 Total autonomic flexibility — captures both sympathetic and parasympathetic contributions to long-range R-R variability. The "operating range" of the ANS. Unlike RMSSD, SD2 remains valid during slow breathing. It is the primary within-session flexibility marker in this dataset. S10 peak (SD2=241 ms, P1) is the clearest single data point in the study. Reduced SD2 found in chronic stress, burnout, and depression (Laborde et al. 2017). A wide SD2 = the nervous system can move freely between states. Narrow SD2 = locked into a fixed arousal level (can be high or low). When high: a sense of spaciousness, inner room — able to be both alert and calm within the same session. When low: "locked in" to a state, less able to shift; not necessarily bad if it means deep, sustained focus. Valid across all stages. Primary within-session metric.
SD1/SD2
Poincaré ratio · unitless		 Balance between beat-to-beat (SD1, PNS) and long-range (SD2, SNS-mediated) variability. High = parasympathetic dominance. Low = sympathetic activation or loss of variability. Provides a directional indicator of sympathovagal balance without frequency-domain analysis. Healthy resting ~0.3. Drops during activation (S4: 0.479 → approaches SNS range). Used in biofeedback and stress research as a real-time balance indicator. Chronic low SD1/SD2 ratio associated with autonomic imbalance and cardiovascular risk. When high: soft, receptive — jaw relaxes, shoulders drop, easier to be moved by music. When low: alert, energised, or slightly on-edge — ready for action, less receptive. Valid across all stages. Interpret alongside SD2 for fuller picture.
DFA α1
Detrended Fluctuation Analysis · exponent		 Short-range fractal scaling of the R-R series (scales 4–16 beats). Measures whether the heartbeat has self-similar temporal structure (~1.0) or is losing that organisation (<0.75). The only nonlinear metric in the set. Uniquely sensitive to loss of autonomic regulation at SNS peaks. S4 (α1=0.727) and S10 (α1=1.043) are the clearest markers of the autonomic arc. Less affected by slow breathing than RMSSD. DFA α1 <0.75 associated with cardiac arrhythmia, overtraining syndrome, and acute autonomic dysregulation (Goldberger 2002, Peng 1995). Healthy resting ~1.0. Rigid/elderly >1.5. Used in sport science to detect non-functional overreaching before it becomes overtraining. Around 1.0: flow, timelessness — the heartbeat organises itself effortlessly. Below 0.75: maximum energy, minimum order — like the moment before a wave breaks. Above 1.3: deep rest or rigidity — the system is no longer adapting, just sustaining. ⚠ Needs ≥150 beats for reasonable estimates (Peng 1995). Slow breathing can also lower α1 artificially — ambiguous without respiratory rate data.

Why not LF/HF power? The LF/HF ratio (standard frequency-domain HRV) requires stationary breathing, minimum 5-min windows, and reliable respiratory rate data. None of these are guaranteed in innerdance. LF power at 0.1 Hz is the target metric for HRV-biofeedback (Lehrer & Gevirtz 2014) but requires resonance-frequency breathing (~6 bpm) to be interpretable — innerdance breathing varies freely. LF/HF should be considered in a future protocol where breathing rate is measured concurrently.

⚠ DFA α1 Confound: Slow Breathing Mimics SNS Activation

DFA α1 is ambiguous below ~0.75 whenever breathing is slow. Two physiologically opposite states — true SNS activation and passive slow breathing — produce the same reduced α1 signal:

Exact confound threshold: Breathing ≤ 9 bpm (0.15 Hz) places the dominant RSA peak at or below the lower boundary of the HF band. At ≤ 6–7 bpm — resonance-frequency breathing as practiced in HRV biofeedback (Lehrer & Gevirtz 2014) — RSA is fully in the LF band and DFA α1 can drop to values indistinguishable from true autonomic dysregulation. This is confirmed by P1's paper-reading window: DFA α1 = 0.630 while cognitively calm and seated, solely because slow reading pace lowered breathing rate into the confound zone.

Innerdance context: Innerdance participants commonly breathe slowly and deeply — particularly in Stages 1–3 (settling), Stage 5 (disintegration), and Stages 10–12 (deep rest). In these stages, a DFA α1 below 0.75 may reflect parasympathetic deepening + slow RSA, not SNS overload. Stage 4 is the one stage where the combination of fast music, movement, and emotional release plausibly shifts the ANS into true sympathetic activation — making DFA α1 interpretable there as genuine regulatory load.

Resolution — concurrent respiratory measurement: A chest-belt (≈€50) or nasal thermistor resolves the ambiguity. Alternatively, the Polar H10's built-in 3-axis accelerometer can derive breathing rate from chest wall movement — this is the approach implemented in the dont-hold-your-breath package (Brennan 2024, github.com/kieranabrennan/dont-hold-your-breath), which uses Butterworth-filtered accelerometer magnitude + peak detection. Our current HRV-based breathing rate estimate (HF band PSD peak from the RR signal itself) is the best available single-sensor proxy; it is shown in the HRV table and stage cards below, colour-coded red where < 9 bpm (confound zone), amber for 9–11 bpm (borderline), and green for ≥ 12 bpm (unambiguous). DFA α1 values in red/amber stages should be interpreted with caution.

📐 Breathing Rate Estimation — HF Band PSD Method

Breathing rate is estimated from the R-R interval series itself, using the respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) method: the lungs modulate heart rate in synchrony with breathing, producing a periodic oscillation in the RR series at exactly the respiratory frequency. Peak detection in the HF power spectral density of the resampled RR signal recovers this frequency.

  1. Clean RR intervals are cubic-spline resampled to a uniform 4 Hz grid.
  2. Welch PSD is computed (nperseg = min(N, 256); ~60 s frequency resolution).
  3. The dominant peak within the HF band (0.15–0.40 Hz) is detected.
  4. Peak frequency × 60 → breathing rate in breaths per minute (bpm).

Comparison to accelerometer-based approaches: The dont-hold-your-breath package (Brennan 2024) uses the Polar H10's 200 Hz 3-axis accelerometer as a respiratory effort signal, applying Butterworth bandpass filtering (0.04–0.5 Hz) and peak detection on the acceleration magnitude. This directly measures chest wall displacement and is independent of the RR signal — making it more robust when RSA is weak (e.g., at very low HR). The two methods are complementary: HF PSD is available with any heart rate monitor; accelerometer-based requires Polar H10 raw data export. Both agree within ~1–2 bpm at normal breathing rates.

Limitations of the HF PSD estimate: (1) When breathing < 9 bpm, the RSA peak moves below the HF band — the estimate returns the HF band boundary value (9 bpm) or may detect a harmonic rather than the fundamental. (2) Very short windows (< 60 s) reduce frequency resolution, making the peak estimate noisier. (3) During movement or strong emotion (Stage 4), irregular breathing may produce a broad PSD peak rather than a sharp peak, inflating the uncertainty. Per-stage estimates use the full stage window (240–420 s), which provides adequate resolution.

Comparison with hrv-analysis package (Aura Healthcare): The hrv-analysis library (github.com/Aura-healthcare/hrv-analysis) computes RMSSD and frequency-domain HRV power bands but does not implement DFA α1 or breathing rate estimation. Our pipeline is more complete for this application. The Aura library's asymmetric Kamath ectopic filter (increases flagged at >32.5%, decreases at >24.5%) is slightly more conservative on upward deviations than our symmetric ±20% rolling-median filter — a minor difference unlikely to affect results with the Polar H10 clean signals used here.

Research Hypotheses

Hypotheses derived from the current data, the acoustic feature set, and the HRV-B literature. Each is stated as a falsifiable prediction about how specific acoustic features of new sound samples impact R-R interval-derived HRV and — where applicable — general health outcomes not measurable by RR alone. Priority is assigned by the intersection of potential impact (clinical or practical value if confirmed), testability (how much additional data/design is needed), and prior likelihood (support from literature or current data).

TIER 1 — Test in next data collection round TIER 2 — Test in expanded study (N≥15, additional sensors) TIER 3 — Future research (methodological investment required)
TIER 1 — High impact · High testability · Moderate-to-high prior likelihood

These can be addressed by adding timestamps, a 5-min post-session rest window, and a brief wellbeing questionnaire to the existing protocol. No new sensors required.

H1 — Acoustic energy arc predicts the autonomic arc
TIER 1

Statement: Sessions structured with an acoustic energy arc (low → peak ~S4 → low → S10 quiet) produce a measurable autonomic arc: DFA α1 drops below 0.85 at the energy peak and recovers above pre-session baseline DFA α1 by the final stage. The arc is absent in sessions without the energy ramp-down.

Acoustic features:
RMS energy (primary), bass energy (secondary)
HRV metrics:
DFA α1 per stage, SD2 at final stage vs. baseline
Required design:
Per-stage timestamps for all participants; 5-min post-session rest window; N≥10
Key confound:
Stage order is fixed — energy arc confounded with time-on-task; need counterbalanced subset
Critical assessment: Directional support from P1 (S4: DFA α1=0.727; S10: 1.043). However the 1.1 ms RMSSD "overshoot" is within noise (see Critical Review). DFA α1 is the stronger metric to test. The hypothesis is testable but requires at minimum a 5-min post-session rest window to establish a meaningful recovery reference. Prior likelihood: medium-high.
H2 — Within-session RMS energy is negatively correlated with DFA α1
TIER 1

Statement: Across stages within a session, RMS energy of the concurrent track is negatively correlated with DFA α1 (Spearman ρ < −0.4, p < 0.05 at N≥10 participants). Higher acoustic energy consistently drives DFA α1 toward the loss-of-regulation zone (<0.75). This holds after controlling for stage position (i.e. it is the energy, not just elapsed time).

Acoustic features:
RMS energy (tested), bass energy (secondary predictor)
HRV metrics:
DFA α1 per stage
Required design:
Per-stage HRV + timestamps, N≥10; partial correlation controlling for stage index
Key confound:
Bass energy and RMS are strongly correlated (r≈0.99 in current data) — multicollinearity prevents separating their contributions
Critical assessment: P1 data shows the correct direction: S4 (highest energy/bass) has lowest DFA α1 (0.727); S10 (quiet, low energy) has highest DFA α1 (1.043). However only 9 data points exist at N=1, and bass energy (r=0.97 with RMS) cannot be separated from overall loudness with this track set. Needs tracks designed to decorrelate bass from total energy. Prior likelihood: medium.
H3 — Pre-session HRV baseline moderates response magnitude
TIER 1

Statement: Participants with higher pre-session DFA α1 (≥0.9) show larger within-session delta HRV at both the activation peak (larger DFA α1 drop at S4) and the recovery peak (larger SD2 increase at S10) than participants with lower pre-session DFA α1. The session "uses" available autonomic range — participants with compressed range show attenuated arcs.

Acoustic features:
None specifically — tests individual moderator of response
HRV metrics:
Pre-session DFA α1 as predictor; Δ DFA α1 and Δ SD2 as outcomes
Required design:
5-min pre-session rest window for all participants; per-stage timestamps; N≥10
Key confound:
Age and fitness are correlated with baseline HRV; sample is too small to separate these
Critical assessment: Consistent with HRV-biofeedback literature (Lehrer & Gevirtz 2014): resonance is larger when there is more autonomic range to engage. P1 (highest baseline HRV) shows largest Δ; P3 (lowest baseline) shows smallest Δ — consistent but confounded by age/fitness. Directionally well-supported; requires controlling for confounders. Prior likelihood: high.
H4 — PNS rebound magnitude predicts post-session subjective wellbeing
TIER 1

Statement: Participants showing a larger increase in DFA α1 between the activation peak (S4) and the recovery peak (S10) report greater post-session improvement on a subjective wellbeing measure (PANAS positive affect, or SUDS 0–10 scale). The physiological rebound and the felt shift track together.

Acoustic features:
S4 vs S10 energy contrast (acts as acoustic manipulation)
HRV metrics:
DFA α1 (S10) − DFA α1 (S4); alternatively SD2 (S10) − SD2 (S4)
Required design:
Add PANAS or SUDS immediately pre and post session; per-stage timestamps; N≥10
Key confound:
Expectation effects (all participants know they're in a "healing" session); needs placebo condition or blind design
Critical assessment: Strongly supported by the mood-HRV literature (Thayer et al. 2012): higher cardiac vagal tone predicts better emotion regulation and positive affect. Adding two 5-min PANAS administrations costs almost nothing. This is the highest-value addition possible given existing protocol. Prior likelihood: high.
TIER 2 — High impact · Medium testability · Requires new acoustic features or additional sensors

These require either new acoustic feature computation, additional hardware (breathing belt), or a larger and more carefully controlled study design.

H5 — Sub-bass (20–80 Hz) drives somatic activation independently of loudness
TIER 2

Statement: Sub-bass energy (20–80 Hz, felt as chest/body vibration) predicts DFA α1 reduction above and beyond overall RMS energy. Two tracks matched on total RMS but differing in sub-bass content will produce different DFA α1 responses. This is distinct from the conscious loudness perception and operates via the vestibular and somatosensory systems.

New acoustic feature required: Split the current bass band (20–250 Hz) into sub-bass (20–80 Hz) and mid-bass (80–250 Hz). Sub-bass at high SPL produces tactile sensation through bone conduction and vestibular activation (Todd & Cody 2000) — a mechanism entirely absent from the mid-bass and above.

Required design:
Controlled tracks with matched RMS but variable sub-bass; in-lab setup with subwoofer to measure actual SPL at body
Key confound:
Sub-bass and overall bass are correlated in this track set; headphone playback eliminates sub-bass entirely
HRV metrics:
DFA α1 per stage; consider also skin conductance (non-RR marker)
Prior likelihood:
Medium. Sub-bass → vestibular → arousal pathway is mechanistically plausible but has not been studied specifically in HRV context.
Critical note: In the current dataset, sub-bass and RMS energy are nearly perfectly correlated (r≈0.99). This hypothesis cannot be tested with the existing tracks — it requires purpose-designed stimuli. Do not report sub-bass as an independent predictor based on current data.
H6 — AM modulation at respiratory frequency entrains breathing and raises SD2
TIER 2

Statement: Tracks with amplitude modulation peaking at 0.15–0.25 Hz (one complete swell every 4–7 seconds) pace participant breathing toward resonance frequency (~0.1 Hz), producing a measurable LF HRV peak and higher SD2 compared to tracks with no periodic AM or AM at other frequencies. This is the HRV-B resonance mechanism applied to music.

New acoustic feature required: AM rate spectrum — compute the power spectrum of the Hilbert envelope (already extracted) and identify the dominant modulation frequency. Current AM depth metric captures the amount of modulation but not its rate. S11+12 has high AM depth; identifying whether its modulation rate falls in the respiratory band is the key test.

Required design:
Breathing belt or nasal thermistor to measure respiratory rate; LF HRV analysis requires 5-min windows
Key confound:
Participants may not synchronise breathing to AM; the entrainment is passive, not instructed — compliance is unknown
HRV metrics:
SD2; LF power (Hz); respiratory rate; SD1/SD2 (paced breathing shifts this ratio predictably)
Prior likelihood:
High mechanistically (Lehrer & Gevirtz 2014; Nozaradan 2011). Unknown whether passive music-driven entrainment is strong enough without instruction.
Critical note: This is the most mechanistically grounded hypothesis in the set — the HRV-B resonance mechanism is well-established. The open question is whether music naturally paces breathing without instruction. S11+12 (high AM depth, highest SD2 trend in P2/P3) is consistent. Adding a breathing sensor is the single highest-value instrumentation upgrade.
H7 — Vocal presence produces higher SD1/SD2 than instrumental tracks at equivalent energy
TIER 2

Statement: Stages containing recognisable human vocal elements (chanting, toning, singing) produce higher SD1/SD2 ratios than instrumental stages at matched energy levels. The polyvagal prosodic pathway (safe/social nervous system) is activated by voice frequencies in the human speech band (85–255 Hz fundamental + 2–5 kHz formants), independently engaging the vagal brake.

New acoustic feature required: Vocal presence classifier — binary or continuous (e.g. ratio of energy in the human voice fundamental frequency range vs. total energy). librosa's harmonic-percussive separation + pitch tracking in the vocal range could approximate this. Alternatively a pretrained vocal activity detector (e.g. pyannote.audio).

Required design:
Annotate which stages contain vocal elements; matched-energy comparison; N≥15
Key confound:
Vocal content is correlated with stage position (e.g. S11+12 reintegration often uses chanting); hard to decorrelate from stage-specific effects
HRV metrics:
SD1/SD2 ratio (most sensitive to acute PNS changes); DFA α1
Prior likelihood:
Medium. Polyvagal theory (Porges 2011) predicts this, but direct music-vocal HRV evidence is sparse.
H8 — Spectral brightness independently predicts SNS activation above an alerting threshold
TIER 2

Statement: Stages with spectral centroid above ~1100 Hz show measurably lower DFA α1 and higher HR than stages with centroid below 700 Hz, at matched RMS energy levels. The auditory startle / alerting reflex is primarily driven by high-frequency content; brightness is an independent ANS activator beyond loudness.

Note on current data: S8+9 has the highest centroid (1431 Hz) and among the highest energy — the two features co-vary and cannot currently be separated. New tracks with high brightness / low energy and low brightness / high energy are needed.

Key confound:
Brightness (spectral centroid) is correlated with energy in most natural music; need purpose-designed stimuli to decorrelate
Prior likelihood:
Medium-high. Auditory startle literature (Khalfa et al. 2002; Leman et al. 2005) supports brightness-arousal link but primarily for sudden onsets, not sustained spectral character.
Required design:
Matched-energy tracks varying only in spectral content; within-participant crossover
H9 — Next-night sleep quality improves following full autonomic arc sessions
TIER 2

Statement: Participants who show a complete autonomic arc (DFA α1 drop ≥0.15 at S4 AND DFA α1 recovery ≥0.15 at S10 relative to S4) report better subjective sleep quality (PSQI or single-item) and/or wearable-measured sleep efficiency the following night compared to their own average, and compared to participants with incomplete arcs. This links the within-session regulatory event to a general health outcome beyond HRV.

Required design:
Next-morning sleep quality questionnaire (PSQI or single item); optional wearable (Oura, Garmin) for objective sleep; N≥15
Key confound:
Many factors affect sleep; needs baseline sleep quality and comparison to personal average, not population norm
HRV link:
Parasympathetic nervous system recovery measured by HRV is a known predictor of sleep initiation and deep-sleep maintenance
Prior likelihood:
Medium. HRV-sleep literature is strong (Stein & Pu 2012). The specific innerdance→arc→sleep pathway is novel.
TIER 3 — High potential · Low near-term testability · Requires methodological innovation

These are directionally important hypotheses that define the medium-term research agenda but cannot be meaningfully tested with current resources and N.

H10 — Acoustic tipping points: feature combinations that reliably cross DFA α1 <0.75
TIER 3

Statement: There exist identifiable threshold surfaces in the acoustic feature space (combinations of RMS energy + bass + brightness) above which DFA α1 consistently drops below 0.75 (regulatory load zone) across participants. Mapping this surface would enable a real-time DJ alert: "this track combination will push the group past the regulatory boundary in ~N minutes."

Why Tier 3: Requires N≥50 sessions to map a 3-dimensional threshold surface with adequate precision. Current N=3 per-stage provides 27 observations — far short of the required density. Additionally, the threshold is likely individual-specific (P1 crosses at DFA α1=0.727; P3 never crosses 0.75 in this dataset). A population-level threshold may not be meaningful unless expressed as a percentile shift from individual baseline.

H11 — Session order is irreplaceable: the arc is the therapy, not any single track
TIER 3

Statement: The PNS rebound at S10 is significantly larger when preceded by the full S1→S9 acoustic arc than when S10 is presented as the first track (or in randomised order). The recovery is potentiated by prior activation — the body needs to be "spent" before it can rebound. Randomising track order would eliminate or reduce the autonomic arc.

Why Tier 3: Requires a crossover design where the same participants experience both ordered and randomised track sequences on separate occasions (minimum 1-week washout). This is the most important structural hypothesis for validating the innerdance protocol design — but it is also the most logistically and ethically complex, as it disrupts the therapeutic sequence.

Critical note: Until this hypothesis is tested, the entire causal model is underspecified. We cannot rule out that each track works independently, or that time-on-task alone explains the apparent arc. This is the single most important experiment for the research programme.
H12 — Salivary cortisol reduction following full-arc sessions
TIER 3

Statement: Participants completing a full innerdance arc (confirmed by DFA α1 drop + recovery) show a statistically significant reduction in salivary cortisol post-session versus pre-session, and versus a matched waiting-room control condition. This is the gold-standard biological validation of the stress-reduction mechanism and would satisfy requirements for clinical research funding.

Why Tier 3: Salivary cortisol requires careful timing (peak ~20 min post stressor), lab-grade storage and assay, and typically N≥30 per group for adequate power. It is the most expensive and logistically demanding test in the set. The expected effect size (based on meditation/music therapy literature) is d≈0.5, requiring N≈50 per group for 80% power at α=0.05.

Principal Data Scientist Summary

Highest priority for next round (Tier 1): Add per-stage timestamps for all participants, a 5-min pre/post resting window, and PANAS/SUDS questionnaires. This unlocks H1–H4 with minimal cost and converts this POC into a publishable pilot study.

Highest methodological value (Tier 2): A breathing belt (≈€50) transforms the interpretability of DFA α1 throughout — it resolves the critical ambiguity between slow-breathing artifact and genuine SNS/PNS state, and enables the respiratory-entrainment hypothesis (H6), which has the strongest mechanistic grounding in the HRV-biofeedback literature.

Non-negotiable for causal claims (Tier 3): H11 (order effect) must be tested before any claim about acoustic features causing HRV changes can be made. Without it, every result in this report is equally consistent with "music matters" and "time-on-task + expectation matters."

Acoustic features to add in next extraction pass: (1) AM rate spectrum — dominant modulation frequency of Hilbert envelope; (2) sub-bass energy split (20–80 Hz vs. 80–250 Hz); (3) vocal presence score (pyannote.audio or harmonic tracking in 85–255 Hz band). All three are extractable from existing audio with no new recordings required.

Overall Assessment

What is reliable: P1's within-session HRV shows a plausible autonomic arc using the correct metrics (DFA α1, SD2) for within-session inference. The audio feature extraction pipeline is technically sound. The qualitative Low/Medium/High framework is a reasonable starting point for expert validation. The respiratory-sinus artifact identification during paper-reading is a genuine, well-supported finding.

What needs caution: No causal claim about music→HRV is supportable yet. Cross-participant absolute HRV comparisons are not valid. DFA α1 interpretation requires respiratory rate context (slow breathing and SNS activation produce the same signal). Short-window DFA estimates have wider uncertainty than full-session estimates.

Recommended reframe: Present this report as a feasibility study and hypothesis-generation instrument, not as evidence of acoustic-HRV causality. The H5 "rebound overshoot" claim should be downgraded to "trend consistent with H5." The value of this POC is the validated pipeline, the expert validation framework, and the identification of the key confounds to address in the next data collection round.
