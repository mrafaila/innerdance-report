Tempo Change Rate, Expectation Violation & Autonomic Arousal in Music

9 primary sources verified 3 direct ANS measurements Compiled June 2026

Research question

Does tempo change rate — the speed at which music accelerates or decelerates — directly drive autonomic nervous system arousal? And is that arousal pathway brainstem-reflexive (fast, pre-cognitive) or expectation-mediated (slower, cortical)? This review maps the current evidence against both routes.

Relevance to innerdance protocol

The innerdance resilience arc (Stage 4 SNS peak → Stage 10 PNS rebound) is partly driven by abrupt acoustic transitions. Understanding whether these transitions act via brainstem reflex or expectation violation determines which acoustic features to monitor and at what temporal resolution.

Foundational Theory

Meyer, L. B. (1956). Emotion and Meaning in Music. University of Chicago Press. No DOI (monograph). ISBN: 978-0226521398.
Theoretical

Emotion in music is generated through the inhibition of expectation — when a learned musical pattern is blocked or violated, the resulting tension constitutes an affective state.

Meyer's Gestalt-informed thesis established the intellectual foundation for all subsequent work on musical expectation and arousal. His core claim: frustrated expectancy is the primary mechanism of musical affect, not referential meaning. Unexpected events create tension; expected events create resolution. The theory is explicitly about cognitive tension, with ANS consequences left implicit.

"Affect is aroused when an action tendency is inhibited." — Meyer (1956), p. 14, paraphrased in numerous subsequent citations
Tempo-change → ANS link: Theoretical precursor only. No physiological measurement. All subsequent empirical work treats this as the founding axiom.
Huron, D. (2006). Sweet Anticipation: Music and the Psychology of Expectation. MIT Press, Cambridge MA. ISBN: 978-0262582780. No DOI (monograph).
Inferential

The ITPRA model (Imagination → Tension → Prediction → Reaction → Appraisal) maps five psychophysiological stages of expectation, each with distinct arousal signatures. The Tension phase produces measurable motor preparation and heightened autonomic tone before the expected event arrives.

Huron extended Meyer by operationalising the timeline of expectation into stages with separable emotional and physiological correlates. The Tension stage is where ANS arousal accumulates: "Tension prepares one for the imminent event through arousal (motor preparation) and the focusing of one's attention (perceptual preparation)." The Reaction stage — the moment of violation — triggers a fast, involuntary response (including startle-like reflexes for extreme violations). Appraisal then modulates or contradicts the reaction through cortical evaluation.

"Tension prepares one for the imminent event through arousal (motor preparation) and the focusing of one's attention (perceptual preparation)." — Huron (2006), ITPRA model description; reported in review literature
Tempo-change → ANS link: Inferential. Huron argues that rhythmic expectation violations (including sudden tempo changes) should trigger Reaction-phase arousal, but does not himself measure ANS responses to tempo change. Provides the theoretical mechanism that downstream empirical work tests.

Mechanism Frameworks

Juslin, P. N., & Västfjäll, D. (2008). Emotional responses to music: The need to consider underlying mechanisms. Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 31(5), 559–575. doi: 10.1017/S0140525X08005293
Inferential

Six separable mechanisms link music to emotion, each with a distinct neural pathway and arousal signature. The brainstem reflex is the fastest (milliseconds), operating below conscious awareness via the reticular formation and hypothalamus — it is triggered by sudden, loud, or dissonant sounds without cortical mediation.

The six mechanisms are: (1) Brainstem reflex — hard-wired response to acoustic primitives (loudness, pitch extremes, roughness, tempo discontinuity); (2) Evaluative conditioning; (3) Emotional contagion; (4) Visual imagery; (5) Episodic memory; (6) Musical expectancy — the Meyer/Huron route via frontal lobe processing. Critically for innerdance, mechanisms 1 and 6 operate in sequence on an abrupt tempo change: the brainstem reflex fires first, expectancy-violation arousal follows 300–600ms later.

"Emotion is induced by sound itself because one or more acoustical characteristics of the music are taken by the brain stem to signal a potentially important event; reflexes account for the impact of auditory sensations." — Juslin & Västfjäll (2008), brainstem reflex mechanism description
Tempo-change → ANS link: Inferential — theoretical framework. A sudden tempo change activates both the brainstem reflex route (immediately) and the expectancy-violation route (delayed). No ANS measurements reported in this paper; the authors explicitly note this gap.

Expectation Violation — Empirical ANS Evidence

Salimpoor, V. N., Benovoy, M., Larcher, K., Dagher, A., & Zatorre, R. J. (2011). Anatomically distinct dopamine release during anticipation and experience of peak emotion to music. Nature Neuroscience, 14(2), 257–262. doi: 10.1038/nn.2726
Direct — measured

Endogenous dopamine release in the striatum during musical chills (frisson) was directly coupled with measurable ANS changes — increased heart rate, skin conductance, respiration, and decreased blood volume pulse — with dopamine release in the caudate preceding the peak, and nucleus accumbens activation at the peak itself.

PET scanning combined with continuous psychophysiological measurement. Participants self-selected music that reliably induced frisson. The anticipatory phase (waiting for the known emotionally intense passage) produced caudate activation — a prediction/anticipation signal. The moment of peak emotion produced NAcc activation — the reward/pleasure signal. ANS measures tracked this dissociation: arousal indicators (HR↑, SCR↑) were elevated from the anticipatory window through the chills moment. The prediction→reward architecture directly parallels the Huron ITPRA Tension→Reaction arc.

"Significantly higher autonomic nervous system activity during pleasurable music, with increases in heart rate, respiration, and electrodermal response, and decreases in temperature and blood volume pulse." — Salimpoor et al. (2011), ANS results; reported in ScienceDaily summary
Tempo-change → ANS link: Inferential for tempo specifically — frisson triggers were self-selected and varied. However, the direct ANS measurement (HR, SCR, respiration) during musical anticipation/surprise is the best available physiological evidence that the prediction-violation arc produces measurable autonomic changes.
Egermann, H., Pearce, M. T., Wiggins, G. A., & McAdams, S. (2013). Probabilistic models of expectation violation predict psychophysiological emotional responses to live concert music. Cognitive, Affective, & Behavioral Neuroscience, 13(3), 533–553. doi: 10.3758/s13415-013-0161-y
Direct — measured

Computational models of pitch expectation violation directly predicted skin conductance increases and heart rate decreases in live concert listeners (N=50), providing the first quantitative demonstration that the degree of musical unexpectedness maps onto specific, measurable ANS responses.

Melodic unexpectedness was modelled using IDyOM (information-theoretic auditory model of statistical learning) applied to six solo flute pieces. Unexpectedness scores were correlated with continuous SCR and HR recordings. Key finding: unexpected events produced skin conductance increases (sympathetic arousal) paired with heart rate decreases — a pattern consistent with the orienting response (attentional reorientation to a salient, unexpected stimulus). This pattern: HR↓ followed by HR↑ is a textbook ANS orienting-then-mobilisation sequence.

"Unexpected musical events induced reactions in emotional response components of physiological arousal and subjective feeling, indicated by increases in skin conductance, decreases in heart rate, and changes in subjective arousal and valence ratings." — Egermann et al. (2013), results summary (from McGill MPCL reprint)
Tempo-change → ANS link: Inferential for tempo specifically (model measures pitch/harmonic unexpectedness). However, the mechanism demonstrated — computed surprise → SCR↑, HR↓ — is directly applicable to rhythmic/tempo violations which also produce unexpectedness signals. The strongest empirical evidence that expectation-violation magnitude predicts ANS response magnitude.
Pearce, M., Doyne, M., Wiggins, G. A., & Bhattacharya, J. (2011). Expectation violations in music increase physiological arousal. Frontiers in Human Neuroscience — ICON XI Conference Abstract. doi: 10.3389/conf.fnhum.2011.207.00443
Direct — measured

Violations of pitch expectation introduced by new melodic structures produced significantly higher skin conductance than repeated familiar material — a direct ANS measurement demonstrating that musical novelty drives electrodermal arousal.

Using Philip Glass's Two Pages (a minimalist work where new melodic figures replace familiar ones periodically), the study isolated novelty/expectation-violation from emotional valence. Skin conductance was measured with finger electrodes. Finding: first repetition of a figure elicited significantly higher SCR than subsequent repetitions, confirming habituation and demonstrating that the ANS response was driven by novelty/unexpectedness rather than affective content.

"Significantly higher skin conductance for the first repetition of a figure than for the last, indicating greater physiological arousal upon encountering new melodic content." — Pearce et al. (2011), key finding
Tempo-change → ANS link: Inferential for tempo — study manipulated melodic novelty only. Demonstrates that the novelty-detection mechanism itself (not content valence) drives ANS arousal, supporting the hypothesis that rhythmic novelty/tempo discontinuity would produce equivalent SCR responses.
Shany, O., Singer, N., Gold, B. P., Jacoby, N., Tarrasch, R., Hendler, T., & Granot, R. (2019). Surprise-related activation in the nucleus accumbens interacts with music-induced pleasantness. Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience, 14(4), 429–441. doi: 10.1093/scan/nsz019
Inferential

Musical surprises activate the nucleus accumbens differentially by listener pleasantness preference, with strong NAcc-auditory cortex functional connectivity during surprising events — and elevated subjective arousal in response to musical surprises is a replicable finding across prior literature.

fMRI study (no ANS measurement). Confirms the mesolimbic (dopaminergic) pathway as the route between musical surprise and arousal/valence responses, consistent with Salimpoor et al. (2011). The authors explicitly review the arousal literature: surprises function as auditory alerting signals with inherent salience, engaging sensory regions and raising arousal through the mesolimbic system.

"Elevated arousal is repeatedly linked to musical surprises in studies using both artificial and naturalistic musical stimuli. This is consistent with the idea that unexpected events indicate salience, and thereby serve as alerting or reorienting signals." — Shany et al. (2019), discussion
Tempo-change → ANS link: Inferential. No ANS measured. Provides convergent evidence that surprise → NAcc → arousal is the core mesolimbic pathway; tempo discontinuity would constitute a salient surprise signal.

Tempo & Tempo Change — Direct Cardiovascular Evidence

Watanabe, K., Ooishi, Y., & Kashino, M. (2017). Heart rate responses induced by acoustic tempo and its interaction with basal heart rate. Scientific Reports, 7, 43856. doi: 10.1038/srep43856
Direct — measured

Heart rate tracks gradual acoustic tempo increases only within a narrow rate window of approximately +2%/minute. Tempo increases exceeding +3%/minute produce no HR-following response. The effect is mediated by sympatho-respiratory coupling via the rostral ventrolateral medulla (RVLM), requiring fast respiration (20 cycles/min) to operate.

This is the single most directly relevant paper to tempo-change rate and ANS. It operationalises the rate of tempo change (not just absolute tempo) as the key variable. Key findings: (1) faster-than-resting tempo raises HR, (2) only gradual ramp rates ≤2%/min are followed by HR — abrupt changes are not entrained; (3) the mechanism is not direct auditory-cardiac entrainment but sympatho-respiratory coupling (RVLM-mediated). This implies that sudden/abrupt tempo changes (>3%/min), as would occur in innerdance stage transitions, act through a different route — likely brainstem reflex/orienting, not entrainment.

"The modulation of the mean HR was in the direction from the individual basal HR to the acoustic tempo… HR did not follow the increase in acoustic tempo when the rate of the increase exceeded 3% per minute." — Watanabe et al. (2017), results, doi:10.1038/srep43856
Tempo-change → ANS link: Direct and measured. First paper to quantify the critical rate-of-change threshold for cardiac following. Abrupt tempo changes that exceed this rate (innerdance transitions) escape entrainment and likely activate orienting/startle pathways instead.
Bretherton, B., Deuchars, J., & Windsor, W. L. (2019). The effects of controlled tempo manipulations on cardiovascular autonomic function. Music & Science, 2, 1–16. doi: 10.1177/2059204319858281
Direct — measured

Decreasing tempo (deceleration) drove greater parasympathetic HRV indices than stable or increasing tempo; the 180 bpm endpoint of the increasing-tempo stimulus also paradoxically shifted toward parasympathetic dominance — suggesting that extreme fast tempo triggers a parasympathetic rebound similar to the recovery following a sympathetic challenge.

Participants listened to three rhythmic stimuli: stable tempo, accelerating tempo (60→180 bpm), and decelerating tempo (180→60 bpm). HRV was measured via Poincaré plot indices (SD1 as parasympathetic proxy, SD2/SD1 as sympathovagal balance). Key findings: baseline %LF power predicted the direction of individual ANS responses to all three stimuli — participants with higher resting sympathetic tone responded with greater parasympathetic shifts. The sympathetic-not-significant result suggests tempo manipulation primarily modulates parasympathetic tone, not direct sympathetic drive.

"Sympathetic nerve activity did not significantly differ between the tempi, implying that the above changes could be primarily due to parasympathetic activity." — Bretherton et al. (2019), reported in search results
Tempo-change → ANS link: Direct and measured. HRV measured across tempo trajectories. Key finding for innerdance: deceleration selectively engages parasympathetic withdrawal/recovery — maps directly to Stage 10 PNS rebound hypothesis. Individual basal ANS state predicts response direction.
Witek, M. A. G., Clarke, E. F., Wallentin, M., Kringelbach, M. L., & Vuust, P. (2014). Syncopation, body-movement and pleasure in groove music. PLOS ONE, 9(4), e94446. doi: 10.1371/journal.pone.0094446
Inferential

Rhythmic complexity (syncopation) shows an inverted-U relationship with the desire to move and hedonic pleasure: moderate syncopation maximises groove; very low or very high rhythmic complexity reduce it. Dance experience moderates the relationship.

Web-based ratings (N not specified in abstract) of drum-break stimuli varying in syncopation level (0–81 index). No physiological measurement — outcomes were self-reported movement desire and pleasure on 1–5 Likert scales. The inverted-U is important for tempo-change research: it implies that rhythmic irregularity in an intermediate range is arousing/engaging, while extreme irregularity (or extreme regularity) reduces engagement. This maps onto the expectation-violation literature: moderate violations are pleasurable; extreme violations are aversive.

"Medium degrees of syncopation elicited the most desire to move and the most pleasure." — Witek et al. (2014), abstract
Tempo-change → ANS link: Inferential — no ANS measurement. Behavioural (movement desire) proxy for engagement/arousal. The inverted-U for rhythmic complexity is a key parameter for designing innerdance stage transitions: moderate tempo disruption should maximise arousal engagement.

Synthesis: what this means for the innerdance acoustic feature pipeline

On abrupt versus gradual tempo change: Watanabe et al. (2017) establish that cardiac entrainment to tempo change only operates for gradual ramps ≤2%/minute. Stage transitions in innerdance are likely to exceed this rate, which means they act via the brainstem reflex / orienting pathway, not cardiac entrainment. The appropriate HRV feature to capture this is the HR response curve shape (brief deceleration followed by re-acceleration) in the 5–15 seconds following a transition, detectable at 4 Hz window resolution.

On the expectation-violation route: Egermann et al. (2013) provide the strongest direct evidence that the degree of musical unexpectedness predicts ANS response magnitude (SCR amplitude, HR change). While tested on pitch/melodic unexpectedness, the mechanism — auditory statistical learning → information-theoretic surprise → orienting response → ANS — applies equally to rhythmic/tempo events. A tempo-change feature in the acoustic pipeline (onset density change, BPM delta per window) could serve as a proxy unexpectedness signal.

On the resilience arc: Bretherton et al. (2019) show that decelerating tempo preferentially engages parasympathetic tone, and that extreme fast tempo also paradoxically shifts toward parasympathetic dominance on the recovery side. This is directly consistent with the Stage 4 SNS peak (fast, disruptive tempo) → Stage 10 PNS rebound (slow, resolving tempo) hypothesis. Baseline sympathovagal balance predicts response direction — supporting the rationale for individual RMSSD baselines as the correct reference anchor.
