Dynamic Attending Theory & Neural Entrainment

4 papers
1999 Inferential

The dynamics of attending: How people track time-varying events

Large, E.W. & Jones, M.R.

Psychological Review, 106(1), 119–159 doi:10.1037/0033-295X.106.1.119

"Attending rhythms are conceptualized as self-sustaining biological oscillations wherein a brief pulse of energy can cause a phase shift, and in behavioral terms, attention is deployed as a series of 'pulses' over time, with perceptual readiness tracking these pulses."

— Described in subsequent review literature citing Large & Jones 1999

Key theoretical claim

Dynamic Attending Theory (DAT) proposes that internal neural oscillators entrain to external rhythmic stimuli, phase-locking attentional pulses to environmental periodicity. This creates anticipatory attentional peaks at expected event times — the foundational mechanism underlying all subsequent beat-entrainment research.

Onset→ANS link

Inferential. DAT addresses attentional and perceptual tracking, not autonomic regulation. The ANS link is downstream: sustained rhythmic attention may modulate arousal through cortico-limbic-autonomic pathways, but this paper makes no direct ANS measurement.

Practical implication for innerdance pipeline

Provides the theoretical scaffold for why onset density could influence ANS state: a rhythm with a salient, stable beat rate sets up entrained attentional oscillators which in turn modulate arousal. Onset density changes that disrupt or deepen entrainment should produce measurable HRV signatures downstream.

2011 Inferential

Tagging the neuronal entrainment to beat and meter

Nozaradan, S., Peretz, I., Missal, M., & Mouraux, A.

The Journal of Neuroscience, 31(28), 10234–10240 doi:10.1523/JNEUROSCI.0411-11.2011

"The beat elicits a sustained periodic EEG response tuned to the beat frequency. Most importantly, we found that meter imagery elicits an additional frequency tuned to the corresponding metric interpretation of this beat. These results provide compelling evidence that neural entrainment to beat and meter can be captured directly in the electroencephalogram."

— Abstract verbatim

Key finding

Using steady-state evoked potentials (SS-EPs), Nozaradan et al. demonstrate that the brain produces a sustained oscillatory EEG response at the exact beat frequency — a direct neural signature of entrainment. Meter (grouping of beats) elicits a separate, selective frequency response even when that frequency is not acoustically present in the stimulus.

Onset→ANS link

Inferential. This paper measures cortical entrainment, not autonomic response. The arousal pathway requires that cortical beat-tracking recruits subcortical structures (thalamus, brainstem nuclei) with autonomic projections — a downstream inference the authors do not test.

2012 Inferential

Selective neuronal entrainment to the beat and meter embedded in a musical rhythm

Nozaradan, S., Peretz, I., & Mouraux, A.

Journal of Neuroscience, 32(49), 17572–17581 doi:10.1523/JNEUROSCI.3203-12.2012

"The amplitude of the SS-EPs obtained at beat and meter frequencies were selectively enhanced even though the acoustic energy was not necessarily predominant at these frequencies."

— Abstract verbatim

Key finding

Extends the 2011 paper: when tempo exceeded the normal beat-perception range, selective neural enhancement disappeared. This establishes a resonance frequency window for entrainment — the onset rate must fall within the perceivable tempo range (~0.5–3 Hz) for beat-selective neural synchronization to occur.

Onset→ANS link

Inferential. Directly relevant: onset density must be in the beat-perceptible range for neural entrainment — and therefore any downstream ANS effect — to operate at all.

2018 Inferential

Neural bases of rhythmic entrainment in humans: critical transformation between cortical and lower-level representations of auditory rhythm

Nozaradan, S., Schönwiesner, M., Keller, P.E., Lenc, T., & Lehmann, A.

European Journal of Neuroscience, 47, 321–332 doi:10.1111/ejn.13826

Key finding

Simultaneous EEG recording from cortical (slow <20 Hz) and brainstem (fast >150 Hz, frequency-following response) sources shows a critical transformation: brainstem responses mirror the acoustic rhythm directly, while cortical responses selectively amplify meter-related frequencies. This identifies brainstem nuclei as the anatomical site where rhythmic entrainment interfaces with subcortical autonomic pathways.

Onset→ANS link

Inferential, but anatomically proximate. The brainstem structures identified (inferior colliculus, cochlear nucleus) are adjacent to reticular and autonomic nuclei. This is the closest neural-entrainment evidence to a direct ANS pathway in the literature reviewed.

Rhythmic Auditory Stimulation & Motor-Autonomic Entrainment

2 papers
2015 Inferential

Neurobiological foundations of neurologic music therapy: rhythmic entrainment and the motor system

Thaut, M.H., McIntosh, G.C., & Hoemberg, V.

Frontiers in Psychology, 5, 1185 doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2014.01185

"Entrainment cues not only changed the timing of movement but also improved spatial and force parameters… Sound has the capacity to prime and time muscle activation via reticulospinal pathways."

— Key findings verbatim

"Entrainment is a temporal locking process in which one system's motion or signal frequency entrains the frequency of another system… entrainment is not defined by beat or phase entrainment — it is defined by period entrainment."

— Definition, key theoretical framing

Key finding

Identifies the reticulospinal pathway as the anatomical route by which auditory rhythm reaches the motor (and potentially autonomic) system. Rhythmic auditory entrainment is period entrainment — the oscillatory cycle, not just beat onset, is what locks. Comprehensive clinical evidence across stroke, Parkinson's, TBI, and cerebral palsy.

Onset→ANS link

Inferential via reticulospinal pathway. The reticular formation contains both motor and autonomic nuclei. Thaut's RAS work focuses on motor, but the pathway is shared. Direct ANS outcomes are not measured in this paper.

2012 Direct

Auditory stimulation and cardiac autonomic regulation

Valenti, V.E., Guida, H.L., Frizzo, A.C.F., Cardoso, A.C.V., Vanderlei, L.C.M., & de Abreu, L.C.

Clinics (Sao Paulo), 67(8), 955–958 doi:10.6061/clinics/2012(08)16

"White noise exposure over 50 dB increases sympathetic activity, and there is a strong correlation between the LF/HF ratio and the noise intensity."

— Verbatim finding on sound intensity and sympathovagal balance

Key finding

Reviews direct HRV measurements showing that auditory stimulation parameters — particularly intensity and spectral content — shift sympathovagal balance measurably. Long-term music therapy improves HRV in cancer patients. Neural mechanism proposed: pleasure response triggers dopamine in striatal system, modulating vagal tone via central autonomic network.

Onset→ANS link

Direct for intensity/spectral content; inferential for onset density specifically. The LF/HF ratio changes with noise intensity are directly measured. The pitch and rhythm effects on HRV are reviewed from other studies but onset density per se is not isolated as an independent variable.

Tempo, Onset Rate & Direct Autonomic Measurement

5 papers
2017 Direct

Heart rate responses induced by acoustic tempo and its interaction with basal heart rate

Watanabe, K., Ooishi, Y., & Kashino, M.

Scientific Reports, 7, 43856 doi:10.1038/srep43856

"Sound stimuli with a faster tempo than the individual basal HR increased the HR… HR did not follow the increase in acoustic tempo [at rates above 3% per minute]."

— Key conditional finding, verbatim

Key finding

Acoustic tempo conditionally influences heart rate and sympathovagal balance (lnLF/lnHF ratio). The relationship is not linear: it depends on whether stimulus tempo exceeds the individual's baseline HR, and on the rate of tempo change (gradual 2%/min works; 3%+ does not). Demonstrates a real but bounded tempo→ANS effect.

Onset→ANS link

Direct, but conditional. Tempo (and therefore beat onset rate) does measurably affect ANS balance — but only within a narrow interaction window with individual baseline physiology. This complicates simple onset density → ANS arousal models.

Practical implication

Onset density as a predictor should be modeled relative to each participant's baseline HR, not as an absolute value. P1's resting HR provides the reference; onset density above that rate should predict sympathetic shift, not onset density per se.

2019 Direct

The Effects of Controlled Tempo Manipulations on Cardiovascular Autonomic Function

Bretherton, B., Deuchars, J., & Windsor, W.L.

Music & Science, 2019 doi:10.1177/2059204319858281

"Shifts towards parasympathetic predominance occurred for the stepped decrease in tempo stimulus but not for the stepped increase… individual tempi impacted upon autonomic function, with 60 bpm in an increasingly slower stimulus associated with greater vagal modulations of heart rate than faster tempi."

— Key findings verbatim from search-extracted abstract

Key finding

Using full HRV frequency-domain analysis (VLF, LF, HF bands) with n=58 participants, controlled tempo decreases shift autonomic balance toward parasympathetic (HF power ↑) but controlled tempo increases do not reliably shift toward sympathetic dominance. The effect is asymmetric and direction-dependent.

Onset→ANS link

Direct; asymmetric. Decreasing onset rate (slowing tempo) has a direct, measurable parasympathetic effect. Increasing onset rate does not reliably produce the mirror sympathetic response. This asymmetry is critical for innerdance stage analysis — SNS activation may require additional cues beyond onset density alone.

2017 Direct

Increase in salivary oxytocin and decrease in salivary cortisol after listening to relaxing slow-tempo and exciting fast-tempo music

Ooishi, Y., Mukai, H., Watanabe, K., Kawato, S., & Kashino, M.

PLoS ONE, 12(12), e0189075 doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0189075

"Salivary oxytocin level was significantly greater after participants listened to slow-tempo piano sequences… salivary cortisol level was significantly lower following fast-tempo music exposure."

— Key neuroendocrine findings, verbatim

Key finding

Slow tempo increases oxytocin and correlates with elevated parasympathetic HRV (↑HF power, ↓HR). Fast tempo reduces cortisol but this does not directly map to sympathetic HRV changes — the cortisol pathway appears dissociated from beat-to-beat autonomic regulation. Proposed mechanism: oxytocin facilitates vagal tone.

Onset→ANS link

Direct for neuroendocrine measures; dissociated from HRV. The slow-onset-rate (low tempo) → oxytocin → PNS activation chain has real measured support. The fast-onset-rate → SNS chain is not confirmed by HRV in this study, reinforcing the asymmetry found by Bretherton 2019.

2024 Direct

Systemic neurophysiological entrainment to behaviorally relevant rhythmic stimuli

Muñoz-Caracuel, M., Muñoz, V., Ruiz-Martínez, F.J., Vázquez Morejón, A.J., & Gómez, C.M.

Physiological Reports, 12(19), e70079 doi:10.14814/phy2.70079

"Active engagement with auditory stimulation synchronizes both CNS and ANS rhythms with the external rhythm… phase angle analysis revealed a consistent alignment across participants between their physiological oscillatory phases at stimulus or response onsets."

— Key finding verbatim

Key finding

The strongest direct evidence for systemic ANS entrainment in this review. Simultaneous measurement of EEG, HR, respiration, EDA, and PPG during active rhythm engagement shows consistent phase alignment across all autonomic channels to stimulus onset. Passive listening does not produce this effect — active engagement is required.

Onset→ANS link

Direct; strongest evidence in this review. Phase locking of ANS oscillations to stimulus onset timing is directly measured. The onset-rate dependency of this effect remains to be established, but the mechanism is confirmed. The active-engagement requirement is important for innerdance sessions — participants are not passive listeners.

2020 Contested

The effect of a rhythmic pulse on the heart rate: Little evidence for rhythmical 'entrainment' and 'synchronization'

Mütze, H., Kopiez, R., & Wolf, A.

Psychology of Music, 2020 doi:10.1177/1029864918817805

"Results showed no evidence for 'entrainment' or 'synchronization' effects of the stimulus on the heart rate… there is no direct and simple correlation between the musical tempo and the heart rate contrary to what is often suggested in everyday psychology."

— Conclusions verbatim

Key finding

Using a real-time feedback paradigm (Djembé beat coupled to actual HR with 25–55% tempo deviation), circular statistics found no frequency entrainment or phase synchronization of heart rate to rhythmic pulse. High interindividual variability. This is the strongest challenge to a simple onset-rate→HR-frequency model.

Onset→ANS link

Contested / negative. Direct HR frequency entrainment (i.e., the HR oscillating at the same frequency as the musical beat) does not appear to occur reliably. This should be distinguished from the broader ANS sympathovagal balance effect — HRV spectral composition can shift without HR frequency locking to the beat.

Practical implication

For the innerdance pipeline: do not expect HR to follow beat frequency directly. The relevant measure is HF power (vagal withdrawal or recruitment) and RMSSD changes over stage windows — not whether HR Hz matches onset rate Hz.

Groove, Syncopation & Sensorimotor Coupling

2 papers
2014 Inferential

Syncopation, body-movement and pleasure in groove music

Witek, M.A.G., Clarke, E.F., Wallentin, M., Kringelbach, M.L., & Vuust, P.

PLOS ONE, 9(4), e94446 doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0094446

"Syncopation predicted the inverted U-shaped relationship better than joint audio entropy… medium degrees of syncopation elicited the most desire to move and the most pleasure."

— Key quantitative finding verbatim

Key finding

Onset pattern complexity — specifically syncopation (onset placement off the metrical grid) — shows an inverted-U relationship with groove: too few syncopated onsets (mechanical, predictable) or too many (chaotic, hard to track) both reduce movement motivation and pleasure. The optimal density is where beat prediction is challenged but not defeated.

Onset→ANS link

Inferential. No autonomic measures were taken (web-based self-report survey). The link to ANS is via sensorimotor engagement and pleasure: groove-inducing rhythms recruit reward circuitry (dopaminergic) which modulates autonomic tone. Onset density pattern — not just rate — affects the quality of entrainment.

Practical implication

For innerdance: the acoustic feature that matters is not raw onset count per second but the syncopation pattern — the timing of onsets relative to the metric grid. Stages 4 (Apoptosis, dense/chaotic) and 10 (Internalization, sparse/regular) may differ in syncopation profile as much as in onset density.

2016 Inferential

Neural entrainment in drum rhythms with silent breaks: Evidence from steady-state evoked and event-related potentials

Stupacher, J., Hove, M.J., Novembre, G., Schütz-Bosbach, S., & Keller, P.E.

Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience, 28(12), 1865–1877 doi:10.1162/jocn_a_01013

Key finding

EEG SS-EPs and EMG from limb muscles measured during drum rhythm listening. Neural (cortical) entrainment to beat persists through silent breaks in the rhythm, indicating internal beat continuation independent of ongoing acoustic onset events. Motor cortex (via EMG) shows correlated excitability rhythms. The 2013 study from this group additionally used TMS to show groove directly modulates motor cortex excitability.

Onset→ANS link

Inferential. Motor cortex excitability is entrained by beat even in absence of onsets — this pathway potentially extends to autonomic regulation via cortico-subcortical projections, but ANS was not directly measured.

Synthesis: What the Evidence Actually Supports

Direct onset→ANS

Weak-to-conditional. Tempo decrease reliably shifts HRV toward PNS dominance (Bretherton 2019; Ooishi 2017). Tempo increase does not mirror this. Direct HR frequency entrainment to beat rate does not occur (Mütze 2020).

Systemic entrainment

Confirmed for active engagement. When participants actively engage with rhythm (not passive listening), ANS phase-locks to stimulus onset timing across multiple channels simultaneously (Muñoz-Caracuel 2024).

Neural mechanism

Brainstem bridge. Beat-selective entrainment occurs at brainstem nuclei (Nozaradan 2018) anatomically adjacent to autonomic nuclei. The reticulospinal pathway is the proposed route (Thaut 2015) — supported but not directly measured for ANS.

Onset pattern vs. rate

Pattern matters more than rate. Syncopation structure (Witek 2014) and beat-frequency range (Nozaradan 2012, 0.5–3 Hz window) constrain the effect more than raw onset counts per second.

For the innerdance HRV pipeline: onset density is a valid predictor of ANS state direction, not magnitude, and only under specific conditions — (1) the onset rate must fall within the beat-perception window; (2) the direction of change (decreasing onset density) more reliably predicts PNS response than increasing onset density predicts SNS response; (3) for SNS arousal (Stage 4, Apoptosis), onset density should be combined with other acoustic features — bass RMS, AM rate, spectral centroid — to build a composite arousal signal. The strongest justification for onset density in a regression model is as a negative predictor of HF power (higher onset density → lower parasympathetic HRV), not as a direct sympathetic activator. The asymmetry in the evidence is itself a finding worth reporting in grant materials.
