This review focuses on peer-reviewed work that reports correlation or regression values between individual spectral audio features — spectral centroid, zero-crossing rate (ZCR), brightness, spectral rolloff, spectral flux — and listener arousal ratings on a dimensional (valence × arousal) model of emotion. The evidence base for this project's acoustic pipeline design: which features carry physiological arousal signal, which carry valence, and where the two are confounded.

01 · Eerola, Vuoskoski & Collaborators

The Eerola / Vuoskoski collaboration produced two empirically distinct lines of work relevant here: perceptual model comparison (which emotion dimensions best describe music ratings) and computational prediction (which audio features predict those ratings). The perceptual papers establish the dimensional space; the prediction papers are where spectral features get tested.

Eerola, T. & Vuoskoski, J. K.
A Comparison of the Discrete and Dimensional Models of Emotion in Music
Psychology of Music, 39(1), 18–49
2011
Role Establishes the stimulus set (110 film music excerpts) and validates the valence × energy-arousal × tension-arousal dimensional space. This is the perceptual foundation that later computational papers build on — not itself a feature-correlation study.
Key finding Three dimensions reduce to two (valence + arousal) without significant loss of fit. The 110-excerpt dataset became a shared benchmark for computational work. Arousal defined as mobilisation of energy, ranging from calm to excited.
Feature data No spectral feature correlations reported. Dimensional ratings only.
Relevance arousal definitionvalence definition — provides the rating labels used in Eerola (2011) and Vempala & Russo (2018).
doi:10.1177/0305735610362821
Eerola, T., Lartillot, O. & Toiviainen, P.
Prediction of Multidimensional Emotional Ratings in Music from Audio Using Multivariate Regression Models
Proc. 10th ISMIR (Kobe), pp. 621–626
2009
Method Multivariate regression (Robust, PLS, PCR) on 110 film music excerpts, predicting valence, energy-arousal, and tension-arousal from a large MIR feature set including spectral centroid, brightness, rolloff, spectral flux, ZCR, plus tempo, mode, and dynamics features.
Arousal (energy) Models explained 77% of variance in energy-arousal ratings. Energy-arousal related primarily to high-to-low frequency energy ratio, spectral centroid, tempo, and RMS energy. Energetic sounds had dominant high-frequency components — spectral brightness is a primary cue.
Arousal (tension) Higher tension carried by brighter sounds, more spectral variation, and gentler attacks. Spectral centroid was a significant predictor for both arousal types.
Valence Models explained 51% of variance in valence. Key clarity and mode were dominant; spectral centroid had a secondary role. Valence is harder to predict from audio than arousal.
Feature ranking spectral centroidbrightness ranked high for both arousal dimensions. modekey clarity dominated valence. ZCR not highlighted as a top predictor.

Eerola, T.
Are the Emotions Expressed in Music Genre-specific? An Audio-based Evaluation of Datasets Spanning Classical, Film, Pop and Mixed Genres
Journal of New Music Research, 40(4), 349–366
2011
Method 39 audio features extracted per excerpt; Multiple Regression and Random Forest tested for cross-genre generalisability of arousal and valence models. Four datasets: classical, film, pop, mixed.
Arousal generalisability 43% of variance explained when testing arousal models across genres. Arousal models generalised moderately across different music types — substantially better than valence.
Valence generalisability Only 16% of variance explained for valence across genres — models failed to generalise. Brightness and spectral centroid contributed to valence predictions within-genre but not cross-genre.
Spectral features Brightness (linked to spectral centroid and spectral entropy) was among the 39 features tested. Brightness predicted arousal across genres better than it predicted valence, but absolute cross-genre R² remained moderate.
Key implication Arousal is the more acoustically predictable dimension. Genre homogeneity matters: models trained on film music (as in innerdance) will not automatically generalise to pop. Within-genre or within-participant models are more reliable.

Vempala, N. N. & Russo, F. A.
Modeling Music Emotion Judgments Using Machine Learning Methods
Frontiers in Psychology, 8, 2239
2018
Method 60 participants, 60 MIDI-rendered music excerpts. 12 audio features extracted: rms, lowenergy, eventdensity, tempo, pulseclarity, centroid, spread, rolloff, brightness, irregularity, inharmonicity, mode. Multiple regression and neural network ensembles.
Arousal correlations Event density: r = 0.48, p < 0.005
Brightness: r = 0.27, p < 0.05
Spectral centroid removed due to multicollinearity with brightness, spread, and rolloff (all r > |0.80|).
Valence correlations Brightness: r = −0.34, p < 0.05
Spectral centroid: r = −0.30, p < 0.05
Event density: r = −0.33, p < 0.05
(All negative — higher spectral brightness correlates with lower valence in this stimulus set.)
Multicollinearity note Spectral centroid, rolloff, spread, and brightness are redundant features — all intercorrelate r > 0.80. Do not include all four in the same regression model. Brightness was retained as the representative timbral feature.
ML performance Neural networks outperformed linear regression. Final models: event density = strongest arousal predictor; brightness = primary valence predictor.

McAdams, S., Douglas, C. & Vempala, N. N.
Perception and Modeling of Affective Qualities of Musical Instrument Sounds across Pitch Registers
Frontiers in Psychology, 8, 153
2017
Method PLSR and neural network models of affective ratings for sustained instrument tones across pitch registers. Features included spectral centroid median, spectral slope, attack characteristics.
Energy arousal Spectral centroid ranked #3 (PLSR) and #5 (neural network) for energy arousal prediction. Pitch register was dominant (r = 0.849 with energy arousal). Higher spectral centroid = greater energy arousal.
Tension arousal Spectral centroid median ranked #1 in neural network for tension arousal. "Higher tension is carried by brighter sounds" — spectral centroid is a direct proxy for perceived brightness.
Valence Valence correlated with pitch register at r = 0.624. Spectral centroid showed a weaker, indirect relationship to valence via brightness. Spectral centroid is primarily an arousal predictor, not a valence predictor, in this study.
doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2017.00153
02 · Broader MIR Literature

Outside the Eerola group, several influential MIR papers independently tested spectral features against arousal ratings. The consistent pattern: arousal is the dimensionally accessible dimension from audio features; valence is harder. Spectral features are more consistently associated with arousal, while mode and key clarity drive valence.

Schubert, E.
Modeling Perceived Emotion with Continuous Musical Features
Music Perception, 21(4), 561–585
2004
Method Continuous real-time emotion ratings from 67 participants listening to 4 Romantic orchestral pieces. Features: loudness, tempo, melodic contour, texture, spectral centroid. Time-series regression with serial correlation correction.
Arousal models Models explained 33–73% of variance in arousal (piece-dependent). Loudness was dominant. Tempo correlated positively with arousal. Spectral centroid did not produce consistent predictions across pieces — its relationship to arousal was piece-specific.
Spectral centroid finding Inconsistent predictor in this continuous-response paradigm. Texture and spectral centroid both showed piece-specific rather than universal effects. This is a key caution: spectral centroid's arousal relationship may depend on musical genre or stimulus type.
Implication for pipeline Loudness (RMS energy) is a more reliable arousal predictor than spectral centroid in continuous within-piece analysis. Use spectral centroid as a secondary arousal feature, not a primary one, in real-time windowed analysis.
doi:10.1525/mp.2004.21.4.561 · Music Perception
Lu, L., Liu, D. & Zhang, H.-J.
Automatic Mood Detection and Tracking of Music Audio Signals
IEEE Transactions on Audio, Speech, and Language Processing, 14(1), 5–18
2006
Method Hierarchical mood detection framework on 800 music clips. Feature sets: intensity (subband energy), timbre (spectral shape + spectral contrast), rhythm (tempo, regularity, strength). Three-dimensional model: arousal, valence, control.
Spectral features Timbre feature set = spectral shape features + spectral contrast. Spectral centroid is implicit in spectral shape. The intensity (subband energy) features — closely related to spectral distribution — were the primary arousal discriminators.
Performance Average mood detection accuracy 86.3%; mood boundary recall 84.1%. Hierarchical approach: arousal first, then valence within arousal class. Confirms arousal is more acoustically predictable.
Key finding Decoupling arousal and valence prediction (sequential rather than joint) improves accuracy. Spectral + intensity features sufficient for arousal; lexical/harmonic features needed for valence.
doi:10.1109/TSA.2005.860344 · IEEE Trans. ASLP
Weninger, F., Eyben, F., Schuller, B. W., Mortillaro, M. & Scherer, K. R.

Frontiers in Psychology, 4, 292
2013

Speech: r = 0.76

Vuoskoski, J. K. & Eerola, T.
Measuring Music-Induced Emotion: A Comparison of Emotion Models, Personality Biases, and Intensity of Experiences
Musicae Scientiae, 15(2), 159–173
2011
Role Compares GEMS, PANAS, and dimensional scales for measuring induced (felt) versus perceived (recognised) emotion. 148 participants, 16 film music excerpts. Dimensional model showed highest intraclass correlation consistency.
Spectral features Not tested. This is a measurement-instrument study, not a feature-prediction study. No spectral feature correlations are reported. Useful context: the dimensional model (valence × arousal) is the most consistent and reliable self-report framework.
Implication If building a ground-truth dataset for training arousal prediction models from HRV or audio, the dimensional model produces more consistent participant ratings than discrete emotion labels or the GEMS.

04 · Key Findings for the Acoustic Pipeline
Finding 1 · Spectral centroid as arousal proxy

Spectral centroid is a reliable but moderate positive predictor of arousal in controlled film music datasets (Eerola et al. 2009: 77% arousal variance; McAdams et al. 2017: ranked top-5 for energy arousal). It is the dominant timbral arousal cue. However, it is highly collinear with brightness, rolloff, and spread — include only one of these four in any regression model. The innerdance pipeline's spectral_centroid feature captures this signal; brightness is effectively redundant if centroid is retained.

Caution 1 · ZCR is not a primary arousal predictor

Zero-crossing rate was not highlighted as a top arousal predictor in any of the reviewed studies. It appears in feature sets (Lu et al. 2006; Weninger et al. 2013) but rarely emerges as a significant regression coefficient for the arousal dimension. ZCR carries textural roughness signal useful for distinguishing sound types, but its relationship to the arousal axis of emotion is weak and inconsistent. Do not weight it heavily in the acoustic-to-HRV model.

Finding 2 · Arousal is more acoustically tractable than valence

Across all reviewed papers, arousal variance explained by audio features consistently exceeds valence: 77% vs 51% (Eerola et al. 2009), 43% vs 16% cross-genre (Eerola 2011). Valence depends heavily on mode, key clarity, and harmonic features not in the current innerdance acoustic feature set. The pipeline's choice to target HF power (a physiological arousal proxy) with spectral features is well-supported — the mapping from audio to arousal is learnable; the mapping from audio to valence is not, with the current feature set.

Caution 2 · Genre specificity limits cross-study inference

Eerola (2011) found that arousal models trained on film music explained only 43% of variance when transferred to pop music. Innerdance uses a distinctive ceremonial sound design (not classical or pop). Feature-to-arousal relationships established in the reviewed literature are directionally reliable but absolute correlation magnitudes will differ. Within-participant or within-session models (Phase 1 OLS in the ML roadmap) will outperform cross-participant models trained on different music corpora.

Finding 3 · Recommended feature priority for arousal prediction

Ranked by evidence strength across reviewed studies: (1) RMS energy / bass RMS — dominant predictor in Schubert 2004 and Weninger 2013; (2) tempo / event density / onset density — r = 0.48 for event density in Vempala & Russo 2018; (3) spectral centroid or brightness (not both) — moderate positive predictor in Eerola 2009, McAdams 2017, Vempala & Russo 2018; (4) spectral flux — moderate in music (r ≈ 0.20–0.28), best as an onset-related feature. ZCR, AM rate, HNR, and silence ratio are secondary features requiring empirical validation on the innerdance-specific corpus.
