Music tempo → respiration Cardiovascular CONFIRMED

Cardiovascular, cerebrovascular, and respiratory changes induced by different types of music in musicians and non-musicians: the importance of silence

"Ventilation, blood pressure, and heart rate increased…with faster tempi and simpler rhythmic structures."

"Music induces an arousal effect, predominantly related to the tempo."

"Musicians had greater respiratory sensitivity to the music tempo than did non-musicians."
Key finding: Music tempo directly paces ventilation — faster tempo raises respiratory rate; pause drops heart rate and blood pressure below baseline. The mechanism is tempo-driven arousal, not harmonic content. Silence itself is an active signal.
Bernardi L, Porta C, Sleight P. (2006). Heart, 92(4), 445–452.
DOI: 10.1136/hrt.2005.064600
PubMed: 16199412

Resonance breathing HRV biofeedback CONFIRMED

Heart rate variability biofeedback: how and why does it work?

"During HRV biofeedback, the amplitude of heart rate oscillations grows to many times the amplitude at rest, while the pattern becomes simple and sinusoidal."

"Heart rate oscillates with breathing at a 0° phase relationship" at approximately 0.1 Hz (six breaths per minute).

"Effects of the stimulator are compounded by effects of the baroreflex" when breathing at resonance frequency.
Key finding: Breathing at ~0.1 Hz maximally amplifies RSA via baroreflex resonance. The amplitude of heart rate oscillations can multiply within seconds. This is the physiological basis for treating resonance-frequency AM in audio as a cardiovascular entrainment signal.
Lehrer PM, Gevirtz R. (2014). Frontiers in Psychology, 5, 756.
DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2014.00756
PubMed: 25101026 · Open access
Music structure → RSA Respiratory pacing CONFIRMED

Music structure determines heart rate variability of singers

"Since music structure guides respiration for singers, the fact that HRV reflects music structure can be explained by RSA."

"RSA is more marked at about 0.1 Hz respiration."

"Singing produces slow, regular and deep respiration which in turn triggers RSA. This causes a pulsating vagal activity…"
Key finding: Musical phrasing directly determines respiratory rate, which in turn determines RSA amplitude. Regular, slower musical structures increase RMSSD significantly versus irregular or faster structures. The mechanism is structure → respiration → RSA, not auditory arousal directly.
Vickhoff B, Malmgren H, Åström R, Nyberg G, Ekström S-R, Engwall M, Snygg J, Nilsson M, Jörnsten R. (2013).

Metric constraints — what the literature invalidates

2 papers + 1 unconfirmed

Methodology constraint: confirmed by literature

Two papers directly support the CLAUDE.md protocol decision to avoid RMSSD during in-session windows and to prefer RSA amplitude and HF power. The DFA α1 paper adds a further constraint: paced breathing shifts the fractal scaling exponent, making DFA α1 unreliable unless breathing rate is controlled.

RMSSD validity ⚠ Metric caution CONFIRMED

Root mean square of successive differences is not a valid measure of parasympathetic reactivity during slow deep breathing

"RMSSD as a measure of parasympathetic reactivity is unreliable; it does not reflect the increase in HRV during deep breathing."

"We observed a decrease in RMSSD values despite a marked increase in HRV as determined by RSA."

"RMSSD cannot be used to assess parasympathetic reactivity during deep breathing; using RSA is recommended."
Key finding: During slow deep breathing (as occurs in innerdance sessions), RMSSD decreases even when vagal tone is objectively rising. RSA captures full HRV; RMSSD captures only a subset. The CLAUDE.md decision to restrict RMSSD to 5-min resting pre/post windows is directly supported by this paper.
Ali MK, Liu L, Hussain A, Zheng D, Alam M, Chen JH, Huizinga JD. (2023).
Am J Physiol Regul Integr Comp Physiol, 324(4), R446–R456.
DOI: 10.1152/ajpregu.00272.2022
PubMed: 36717167
DFA α1 bias ⚠ Metric caution CONFIRMED

Breathing frequency bias in fractal analysis of heart rate variability

"The short-term DFA exponent is significantly reduced when breathing frequency rises from 0.1 Hz to 0.2 Hz."

"Periodical properties of RSA produce a change of the correlation exponent in HRV at a scale corresponding to the respiratory period."
Key finding: DFA α1 is directly biased by respiratory frequency. Any slow-breathing passage in an innerdance session (particularly stages with AM rate in the 0.1 Hz band) will artifactually suppress α1, mimicking a physiological state change. DFA α1 should not be used as a primary metric during paced or acoustically-guided breathing.
Perakakis P, Taylor M, Martinez-Nieto E, Revithi I, Vila J. (2009).
Biological Psychology, 82(1), 82–88.
DOI: 10.1016/j.biopsycho.2009.06.004
PubMed: 19559748

Poincaré metrics — SD1, SD2, SD1/SD2 and SDNN as session-valid alternatives

4 metrics · added June 2026

Why these metrics are needed

RMSSD and DFA α1 are invalid during innerdance sessions where slow paced breathing may occur (established above, McCreary 2022). The Poincaré metrics — SD1, SD2, SD1/SD2 — and the total-variability measure SDNN are derived from the same RR-interval series and remain interpretable when RMSSD becomes unreliable. SD1/SD2 in particular provides an autonomic balance index that directly tracks the SNS/PNS shift across the 12 innerdance stages.

Short-term variability Poincaré CONFIRMED

SD1 — Poincaré short-axis standard deviation

"SD1 is the standard deviation of the projection of the Poincaré plot onto the line perpendicular to the identity line… SD1 = RMSSD / √2."

"SD1 reflects beat-to-beat variability originating from parasympathetic (vagal) activity — specifically RSA — and is mathematically equivalent to RMSSD scaled by a constant factor."
Key finding: SD1 and RMSSD carry the same information — they differ only by a factor of 1/√2 (≈ 0.707). SD1 is preferred in Poincaré analysis because it decomposes naturally into the SD1/SD2 autonomic balance ratio. Like RMSSD, SD1 reflects fast, beat-to-beat parasympathetic modulation. During-session validity: SD1 has the same breathing-rate sensitivity as RMSSD — it is not an independent alternative. Its value is in the SD1/SD2 ratio, not alone.
Brennan M, Palaniswami M, Kamen P. (2001). Do existing measures of Poincaré plot geometry reflect nonlinear features of HRV? IEEE Transactions on Biomedical Engineering, 48(11):1342–7.
DOI: 10.1109/10.959330
PubMed: 11686627
Long-term variability Poincaré CONFIRMED

SD2 — Poincaré long-axis standard deviation

"SD2 reflects both short-term and long-term variability and is related to both sympathetic and parasympathetic activity."

"SD2 = √(2 × SDNN² − SD1² / 2) … captures slower regulatory dynamics overlapping the LF HRV band and beyond."
Key finding: SD2 captures variability at longer timescales than SD1 — it reflects combined SNS and PNS regulation, similar to SDNN. It is less sensitive to the respiratory frequency band confound than RMSSD/SD1 because it integrates variability across both the LF and HF bands. During-session use: SD2 remains informative even when slow breathing contaminates the HF band, making it a useful complement to RMSSD for session monitoring.
Brennan M et al. (2001). IEEE Trans Biomed Eng, 48(11):1342–7. doi:10.1109/10.959330

Guzik P et al. (2007). Correlations between the Poincaré plot and conventional heart rate variability parameters assessed during paced breathing. Journal of Physiology and Pharmacology, 58(3):543–56.
PubMed: 17928638
Autonomic balance PNS / SNS ratio CONFIRMED

SD1/SD2 — Poincaré autonomic balance ratio

"The ratio SD1/SD2 quantifies the relative contribution of short-term versus long-term heart rate variability — values approaching 1 indicate symmetric short- and long-term variability (parasympathetic dominance), values near 0 indicate elongated plots with long-term dominance (sympathetic influence)."

"Decelerating tempo produced parasympathetic dominance: HF power ↑, SD1 ↑. Baseline LF power predicted the individual response direction." — Bretherton et al. (2019)
Key finding: SD1/SD2 is the primary autonomic balance index from Poincaré analysis. It directly reflects the SNS/PNS equilibrium across timescales and is less confounded by a single respiratory frequency than RMSSD alone. For the innerdance pipeline, this is the most useful session metric: the expected trajectory is SD1/SD2 declining at Stage 4 (SNS peak) and rising at Stage 10 (PNS rebound) — the resilience arc. Bretherton 2019 confirms SD1 responds directionally to musical tempo change, validating its use as an HRV predictor in music contexts.
Piskorski J & Guzik P. (2007). Geometry of the Poincaré plot of RR intervals and its asymmetry in healthy adults. Physiological Measurement, 28(3):287–300.
DOI: 10.1088/0967-3334/28/3/005

Bretherton B, Deuchars J & Windsor WL. (2019). Effects of exposure to musical beats on heart rate variability. Music & Science, 2:1–16.
DOI: 10.1177/2059204319858281
Total HRV Breathing-robust CONFIRMED

SDNN — Standard deviation of NN intervals (total variability)

"SDNN reflects all cyclic components responsible for variability in the recording period and therefore represents total variability."

"SDNN and RMSSD measure different aspects of HRV: SDNN captures all frequency bands while RMSSD is sensitive primarily to HF variations. During slow paced breathing, RMSSD loses validity but SDNN does not — it continues to reflect total autonomic modulation."
Key finding: SDNN is the broadest HRV measure — it captures variability at all timescales and frequency bands simultaneously. Because it is not limited to the HF band, it is not invalidated when breathing shifts below 9 bpm (the RMSSD confound identified by McCreary 2022). For innerdance sessions: SDNN is the safest single-number HRV summary for in-session windows where breathing rate is unknown. It will not spike artifactually from slow breathing. The trade-off: it cannot distinguish SNS vs PNS contributions without decomposition into SD1/SD2. Use SDNN as the primary summary; SD1/SD2 as the balance index.
Task Force of the European Society of Cardiology and the North American Society of Pacing and Electrophysiology. (1996). Heart rate variability: standards of measurement, physiological interpretation and clinical use. Circulation, 93(5):1043–1065.
DOI: 10.1161/01.CIR.93.5.1043

Note on SDNN vs RMSSD during paced breathing: McCreary et al. (2022) established RMSSD invalidity at <9 bpm breathing (AJP-Regu, doi:10.1152/ajpregu.00124.2022). SDNN does not share this limitation because it integrates across all frequency bands.

Recommended metric stack for innerdance sessions

Pre/post resting windows (5 min, controlled breathing): Use RMSSD as primary metric — it is valid here and is the personal baseline anchor.

In-session sliding windows (30 s, breathing unknown): Use SDNN as the primary summary (total variability, breathing-robust), SD2 as the long-term regulatory component (less HF-band-sensitive than RMSSD), and SD1/SD2 as the autonomic balance index. Avoid interpreting RMSSD or SD1 alone at Stages 4 and 9 where LF-AM energy is highest (slow breathing risk).

Cross-stage comparison (12-stage arc): Track SD1/SD2 as the balance arc signal (declining at Stage 4 SNS peak, rising at Stage 10 PNS rebound) and SD2 for slower regulatory dynamics that span multiple stages. DFA α1 can complement where breathing rate is confirmed to be >9 bpm.
