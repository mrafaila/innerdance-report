Foundational textbook · Psychoacoustics

Psychoacoustics: Facts and Models

Authors:Fastl, H., & Zwicker, E. Year:2007 (3rd ed.; 1st ed. 1990) Publisher:Springer, Berlin/Heidelberg. Series: Springer Series in Information Sciences. ISBN:978-3-540-23159-2 Chapter:Chapter 11 — Roughness URL:https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-540-68888-4_11
Technical definition of roughness

Roughness arises when a sound undergoes amplitude modulation at rates between 15 and 300 Hz. Below 15 Hz the sensation is of flutter or fluctuation strength; above 300 Hz the modulations fuse into a steady percept with no roughness quality. Perceived roughness peaks at a modulation frequency of ~70 Hz for carrier frequencies above 1 kHz (shifts to ~30 Hz for lower carriers). The unit is the asper: one asper equals a 1 kHz pure tone at 60 dB SPL with 100% sinusoidal amplitude modulation at 70 Hz.

Key findings on arousal and alertness
  • Alarm signal design: engineers empirically converge on the roughness range to enhance signal salience — rough sounds capture attention more efficiently than smooth tones of equal loudness.
  • Unpleasantness coupling: roughness and sharpness are the primary contributors to sensory dissonance and perceived unpleasantness; roughness has the stronger effect of the two.
  • Temporal salience: fast repetitive modulations produce "temporally salient flickering percepts" analogous to strobe lights, efficiently capturing attention and inducing avoidance.
  • Roughness is computed as R = cal · ∫ f_mod · ΔL dz — the modulation frequency multiplies the perceived modulation depth across all critical bands, making f_mod a direct scaling factor in the sensation magnitude.
⬡ANS link: indirect — via attention/avoidance, not direct ANS measurement
Empirical study · Neuroscience

The Rough Sound of Salience Enhances Aversion Through Neural Synchronisation

Authors:Arnal, L.H., Kleinschmidt, A., Spinelli, L., Giraud, A.-L., & Mégevand, P. Year:2019 Journal:Nature Communications, 10, 3671. DOI:https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-019-11626-7 PMC:PMC6694125
Definition of roughness used

"An acoustic texture that arises from fast repetitive acoustic transients" in the 30–150 Hz range. Maximal aversive responses occur in the 40–80 Hz band — the perceptual boundary between discrete events and continuous pitch. Aversion is nonlinear, peaking in this range regardless of stimulus intensity.

Key findings on arousal and neural response
  • Large-scale network synchronization: rough sounds produce "sustained, phase-locked responses at the rate of stimulation" that synchronise beyond auditory cortex into limbic and frontal regions involved in salience and aversion.
  • Specific recruitment: the amygdala, hippocampus, and insula are specifically activated — areas associated with threat processing and aversion, not present for smooth tones.
  • Arousal-aversion coupling: participants with enhanced subjective aversion also show stronger 40 Hz steady-state EEG responses, linking the neural signature to subjective arousal state.
  • The brain cannot suppress repetitions spaced <25 ms apart, maintaining constant alertness to the rough stimulus — the auditory system is structurally biased to remain aroused.
  • Exact quote: "Rough sounds synchronise activity throughout superior temporal regions, subcortical and cortical limbic areas, and the frontal cortex, a network classically involved in aversion processing."
⬡ANS link: indirect — limbic network activation implies downstream ANS engagement; no direct ANS measurement
Empirical study · Behavioral & Cognitive Neuroscience

Auditory Roughness Elicits Defense Reactions

Authors:Taffou, M., Suied, C., & Viaud-Delmon, I. Year:2021 Journal:Scientific Reports, 11, 956. DOI:https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-020-79767-0 PMC:PMC7806762
Definition and stimulus

Roughness was operationalized as amplitude modulation at 70 Hz applied to a harmonic tone with 500 Hz fundamental — matching Fastl & Zwicker's reference standard. The study specifically tested whether roughness constitutes an innate threat cue triggering defensive behavior, independent of learned associations.

Key findings on defense and arousal
  • Expanded peripersonal space: rough sounds speeded tactile reaction times at farther distances from the body than non-rough sounds — indicating the defensive safety zone around participants expanded in response to roughness.
  • Conclusion: auditory roughness "modifies the behavioral relevance of simple auditory events in relation to the body" and constitutes "an innate threat cue that elicits defensive responses."
  • Subjective arousal confirmed: participants rated rough sounds as significantly more negative and more arousing (valence–arousal ratings), even when both rough and non-rough sounds were matched for loudness.
  • The defense reaction is not conditioned — it occurs without prior threat association, consistent with an evolutionarily conserved alarm response mechanism.
⬡ANS link: behavioral defense proxy — no heart rate or SC measured directly; arousal confirmed via self-report
Note: Direct ANS measures (heart rate variability, skin conductance) were not included in this study. The defense response was measured behaviorally via peripersonal space expansion. Arousal was self-reported.
Empirical study · Psychophysics

Acoustic Estimation of Voice Roughness

Authors:Anikin, A. Year:2025 Journal:Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics, 87(5), 1771–1787. DOI:https://doi.org/10.3758/s13414-025-03060-3
Updated technical definition

"Sounds are perceived as rough if the envelopes of cochlear filters contain modulation frequencies between about 15 and 300 Hz." Refines the peak sensitivity estimate to 50–200 Hz (lognormal weighting). Confirms Fastl & Zwicker's 70 Hz reference while narrowing the most perceptually relevant band.

Key findings on arousal link
  • Arousal–valence coupling confirmed: "Rough-sounding noise was associated with higher perceived arousal and more negative valence in music" — direct measurement of the roughness–arousal association.
  • Salience correlation: "Auditory salience strongly correlates with the roughness of stimuli" — roughness is a primary driver of attention capture independent of loudness.
  • Vocal roughness in mammalian alarm calls is argued to be evolutionarily conserved, functioning as a cross-species arousal signal (screams, growls, distress vocalizations).
  • Confirms Fastl & Zwicker's peak: "sensation of roughness is reported to peak at 75–100 Hz for amplitude-modulated tones."
⬡ANS link: indirect — self-reported arousal and salience ratings; no physiological measures
Foundational monograph · Music cognition

Embodied Music Cognition and Mediation Technology

Author:Leman, M. Year:2008 Publisher:MIT Press, Cambridge, MA. ISBN:978-0-262-12293-1 URL:https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262122931/embodied-music-cognition-and-mediation-technology/
Framework description — verification status: partial

Leman argues that the human motor system, gestures, and body movements are the primary mediators of music perception. Arousal is an ensuing feature of embodied sensorimotor predictions — not a direct response to acoustic properties like roughness, but an output of action-perception coupling. The book is not organized around psychoacoustic attributes.

Relevance to roughness and ANS
  • Acoustic features and arousal: Leman treats acoustic features (including consonance/dissonance, related to roughness) as inputs to sensorimotor processing. Arousal emerges from the mismatch or alignment between musical motion and bodily engagement, not from acoustic roughness per se.
  • Dissonance framing: "music is made of combinations of basic melodic intervals that differ by their psychophysical characteristics, among which the level of tonal stability and consonant-dissonant dichotomy are the most important" — this is where roughness enters the framework, as a contributor to dissonance.
  • Physiological responses: EMC assumes "subjective experiences of music are expressed directly by means of measurable corporeal articulation (gestures)" — the body is the measurement instrument for arousal, not peripheral ANS metrics.
  • Honest scope note: Leman 2008 is valuable as a theoretical framework for why acoustic texture (roughness) produces bodily responses, but does not itself measure ANS outcomes or define roughness technically. Cite Fastl & Zwicker and Arnal et al. for mechanism; cite Leman for the embodiment framework that links sound to bodily arousal state.
⬡ANS link: embodied cognition framework — body movement as proxy for arousal; no direct ANS measures
Partial confidence: Leman 2008 addresses acoustic features and arousal but the roughness–ANS link is not the book's central argument. Primary roughness sources are Fastl & Zwicker and the Arnal/Taffou empirical literature.
Empirical study · Human neuroscience

Electrophysiological and Behavioural Responses to Consonant and Dissonant Piano Chords

Authors:Kirsanov, A., Koriakina, M., Panasenko, D., Nikishkina, U., & Blagovechtchenski, E. Year:2025 Journal:Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 19, 1689067. DOI:https://doi.org/10.3389/fnhum.2025.1689067
Dissonance operationalization (roughness-adjacent)

Dissonant chords were constructed to maximise sensory dissonance through proximity of sounds and non-harmonic intervals — the acoustic mechanism of dissonance is roughness (beating between near-frequency partials). ERP (N1, P2, P3a) and EEG band analysis measured neural responses.

Key findings
  • P2 and P3a amplitudes: dissonant chords evoked greater P2 and P3a amplitudes than neutral stimuli, indicating increased attentional and affective salience.
  • Widespread bilateral activation: dissonant stimuli elicited "widespread bilateral frontal and temporal activation suggestive of heightened attentional engagement and emotional salience."
  • Behavioral: "consonant chords were rated as more pleasant" while "dissonant chords were rated as more unpleasant" — the valence asymmetry of roughness reproduced.
⬡ANS link: indirect — EEG measures; no HRV or skin conductance
Synthesis — the roughness–arousal–ANS chain

Evidence chain: from acoustic modulation to autonomic activation

01
Stimulus: any audio signal with amplitude modulation in the 15–300 Hz range produces the percept of roughness. Peak sensitivity at ~70 Hz (Fastl & Zwicker 2007). In innerdance audio, this includes distorted bass, vocal texture, and AM-modulated drones. The AM rate feature extracted by acoustic_processor.py (Hilbert envelope) directly captures this range.
02
Cochlear processing: roughness arises from amplitude-modulation products on the basilar membrane — it is a consequence of peripheral auditory mechanics, not top-down interpretation. The auditory system cannot suppress fast repetitions (<25 ms inter-pulse interval) — sustained roughness produces sustained peripheral drive (Arnal et al. 2019).
03
Large-scale network recruitment: rough sounds synchronise not just auditory cortex but amygdala, hippocampus, insula, and frontal salience networks in a sustained, phase-locked manner (Arnal et al. 2019). Dissonant chords (roughness operationalized musically) reproduce this in EEG as elevated P2/P3a (Kirsanov et al. 2025).
04
Defense/arousal output: roughness constitutes "an innate threat cue that elicits defensive responses" (Taffou et al. 2021). Rough sounds expand peripersonal space (the body's defensive safety zone) and are rated as significantly more arousing than matched non-rough sounds. Arousal–roughness correlation confirmed in music contexts (Anikin 2025).
05
Autonomic gap — what is and isn't established: No study in this review directly measures HRV, RMSSD, HF power, or skin conductance in response to controlled roughness. The chain from limbic activation (step 3) to ANS activation is biologically well-supported but not yet directly demonstrated for isolated acoustic roughness. This is the measurement gap the innerdance pipeline can address by correlating AM_rate windows with in-session HF_power windows.
Implication for acoustic feature selection: AM rate (Hilbert envelope, targeting 15–300 Hz) is the correct acoustic feature to track roughness in innerdance sessions. Correlating this against HF power windows (the in-session ANS proxy) tests the roughness–ANS link directly. This would be a novel contribution — existing literature has established the roughness–arousal perceptual link but not closed the loop to HRV.
