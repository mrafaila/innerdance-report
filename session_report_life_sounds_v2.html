📖 Understanding the HRV Metrics

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) measures the fluctuation in time between consecutive heartbeats (R-R intervals). A healthy autonomic nervous system produces irregular intervals — this variability is adaptive, not noise. Four metrics were chosen because together they cover the time domain, Poincaré geometry, and nonlinear fractal structure of the heartbeat — and each remains interpretable under the specific constraints of innerdance data (short windows, slow breathing, single-sensor Polar H10).

Metric What it measures Why chosen for this study Clinical evidence Subjective experience Validity caveat
RMSSD
Root Mean Square of
Successive Differences · ms		 Beat-to-beat parasympathetic (vagal) tone. Captures how much each heartbeat deviates from the one before it. Gold-standard resting vagal marker; simple to compute; provides the pre-session baseline anchor and the personal RMSSD reference. Requires 5-min resting window (Task Force ESC/NASPE 1996). Higher resting RMSSD associated with lower all-cause mortality (Shaffer & Ginsberg 2017, Front. Public Health 5:258), reduced depression and anxiety risk, better cardiovascular resilience. Age-stratified population norms: median age 40–50 = 38 ms (Nunan et al. 2010, Ann Clin Biochem 47(2):99–111). Athletes typically 60–100 ms; sedentary adults 20–40 ms. When high: chest opens, breathing deepens, a felt sense of ease and safety. When low: a subtle tightening, more rigid rhythm — the body is mobilising, not distressed. ⚠ Invalid during innerdance when breathing <9 bpm — RSA moves out of the HF band, corrupting RMSSD (McCreary et al. 2022, Am J Physiol 323:R519–R531, doi:10.1152/ajpregu.00124.2022). Use only against resting baseline.
SD2
Poincaré long-axis · ms		 Total autonomic flexibility — captures both sympathetic and parasympathetic contributions to long-range R-R variability. The "operating range" of the ANS. Derived from the long axis of the Poincaré plot (Brennan et al. 2001). Unlike RMSSD, SD2 remains valid during slow breathing. It is the primary within-session flexibility marker in this dataset. Stage 10 peak (SD2=241 ms, P1) is the clearest single data point in the study. Reduced SD2 in chronic stress, burnout, and depression (Laborde et al. 2017, Front. Psychol. 8:213, doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2017.00213). Mathematical derivation: Brennan et al. 2001, Ann Biomed Eng 29(9):799–804, doi:10.1114/1.1395573. Normal range: 60–250 ms. When high: a sense of spaciousness, inner room — able to be both alert and calm within the same session. When low: "locked in" to a state, less able to shift; not necessarily bad if it means deep, sustained focus. ✓ Valid across all stages. Primary within-session metric.
SD1/SD2
Poincaré ratio · unitless		 Balance between beat-to-beat (SD1, PNS) and long-range (SD2, SNS-mediated) variability. High = parasympathetic dominance. Low = sympathetic activation or loss of variability. SD1 ≈ RMSSD/√2 (Brennan et al. 2001). Provides a directional indicator of sympathovagal balance without frequency-domain analysis. Healthy resting ≈0.3. Drops during activation (S4: 0.479 → approaches SNS range). SD1 and SD2 geometry: Brennan et al. 2001, Ann Biomed Eng 29(9):799–804, doi:10.1114/1.1395573. Biofeedback + stress research context: Laborde et al. 2017, Front. Psychol. 8:213. Chronic low SD1/SD2 associated with autonomic imbalance and cardiovascular risk. When high: soft, receptive — jaw relaxes, shoulders drop, easier to be moved by music. When low: alert, energised, or slightly on-edge — ready for action, less receptive. ✓ Valid across all stages. Interpret alongside SD2 for fuller picture.
DFA α1
Detrended Fluctuation
Analysis · exponent		 Short-range fractal scaling of the R-R series (scales 4–16 beats). Measures whether the heartbeat has self-similar temporal structure (≈1.0) or is losing that organisation (<0.75). The only nonlinear metric in the set. Uniquely sensitive to loss of autonomic regulation at SNS peaks. S4 (α1=0.727) and S10 (α1=1.043) are the clearest markers of the autonomic arc. Less affected by slow breathing than RMSSD. DFA α1 <0.75 associated with cardiac arrhythmia, overtraining syndrome, and acute autonomic dysregulation. Healthy resting ≈1.0. Rigid/elderly >1.5. Used in sport science to detect non-functional overreaching before it becomes overtraining. Original method: Peng et al. 1995, Chaos 5(1):82–87, doi:10.1063/1.166141; clinical HRV complexity framework: Goldberger et al. 2002, PNAS 99(suppl 1):2466–72, doi:10.1073/pnas.012579499. Around 1.0: flow, timelessness — the heartbeat organises itself effortlessly. Below 0.75: maximum energy, minimum order — like the moment before a wave breaks. Above 1.3: deep rest or rigidity — the system is no longer adapting, just sustaining. ⚠ Needs ≥150 beats for reliable estimates (Peng 1995). Slow breathing artificially lowers α1 — ambiguous without respiratory rate (McCreary 2022). Scale 4–16 beats validated vs. neurokit2 (diff < 0.002).

🎵 Why These Acoustic Features?

These features were selected because each maps onto a distinct dimension of auditory perception known to drive autonomic nervous system responses. Only features with CV ≥ 0.25 (discriminates across stages) and |r| ≥ 0.35 (correlated with at least one HRV metric across this session's stages) are shown, ranked by correlation strength.

Feature Why it matters physiologically What an innerdance expert hears / feels Key literature
Sub-Bass Vibration
RMS amplitude in 20–250 Hz band 		Sub-bass and low-frequency vibration activates the vestibular system and somatic (tactile) receptors in the chest and abdomen. Strong bass is associated with increased HR and SNS activation, and also with trance-induction via repetitive low-frequency entrainment. Felt as physical vibration in the sternum, belly, and legs. Experienced practitioners describe strong bass as 'grounding' or 'destabilising' depending on context. Peak at Stage 7–8. Juslin & Västfjäll (2008) Behav Brain Sci 31(5):559–621; Todd & Cody (2000) J Acoust Soc Am 108(6):2974–82. Vestibular saccular activation: Todd & Cody 2000. Emotion via music: Juslin & Västfjäll 2008.
Sonic Movement / Change
L2 norm of column-to-column STFT difference 		Rate of spectral change over time. High flux = timbral evolution, novel sonic events, transitions. Spectral novelty drives attention and may produce orienting responses (HR deceleration, then return). Does the sound feel like it is constantly changing, evolving (high flux) or sustaining one texture (low flux)? High at S3 and S5; low at S1 (pure drone). Trost et al. (2012) Neuropsychologia; Huron (2006) Sweet Anticipation
Rhythm Clarity
CV of the onset strength envelope (std/mean) 		Metrically clear rhythm (high CV: strong beats punctuate silence) vs. ambient / drone texture (low CV: steady undifferentiated energy). Clear rhythm enables neural beat-locking (motor–auditory coupling) which modulates HR and can sustain SNS activation. Does the track have a clear, identifiable beat (High) or is it a continuous texture where you cannot tap along (Low)? S3 and S11+12 score High via different mechanisms. Grahn & Brett (2007) Neuropsychologia; Nozaradan et al. (2011) J Neurosci
Bass Vibration
RMS amplitude in 20–250 Hz band (broader low-frequency band) 		Broader low-frequency energy — includes sub-bass and upper bass. Complementary to sub_bass_energy; less specific vestibular mechanism but captures overall somatic presence of low-end content. How 'heavy' or 'weighty' does the track feel? Felt in chest and floor. Overlaps with sub-bass but also picks up kick drums and bass instruments. Juslin & Västfjäll (2008) Behav Brain Sci
Tempo Instability
CV of beat-period estimates across overlapping 4 s windows 		Sudden tempo shifts disrupt hippocampal time-cell prediction loops, triggering subcortical/reticular/vestibular activation. The nervous system can't predict 'when next' — this generates arousal independently of volume. Does the rhythm feel unpredictable or unstable? High instability = the track keeps surprising the body's motor system; low = steady hypnotic pulse. Expert feedback June 2026. Beat induction / basal ganglia: Grahn & Rowe (2009) J Neurosci 29(23):7540–8. Neural entrainment: Nozaradan et al. (2011) J Neurosci 31(27):10234–40.
Tonal Brightness
Hz frequency of the spectral centre of mass 		High-frequency content is perceived as 'sharp' or 'harsh' and triggers alerting responses via the auditory brainstem reflex. Correlated with increased skin conductance and cortisol. Low brightness (warm, muffled) is associated with parasympathetic relaxation. The tonal 'colour' of the track — dark and warm (low) vs. bright and piercing (high). S8+9 (highest centroid ≈1431 Hz) sounds metallic and intense; S11+12 (≈378 Hz) sounds warm and rounded. Leman et al. (2005) Musicae Scientiae; Eerola & Vuoskoski (2011) Music Percept
Brightness / Presence
RMS amplitude in 2–8 kHz band 		High-frequency band energy. Bright, present, cutting sound. Correlated with alertness and SNS arousal. H8 hypothesis: brightness above ~1100 Hz independently predicts sympathetic activation. The 'air' and 'presence' of the track — does it cut through and feel immediate? High = sharp, forward, attention-grabbing. Low = recessed, warm. Leman et al. (2005) Musicae Scientiae
Tonal vs. Noise Quality
Ratio of geometric to arithmetic mean of power spectrum 		Near 0 = strongly tonal (pure tone-like). Near 1 = noise-like / white noise. Tonal signals trigger pitch-processing and melodic expectation; noise-like signals activate diffuse arousal systems without melodic expectation. Stage 1 is almost a pure tone (flatness ≈0) — resonant bowl or sine-wave drone. Stages with high flatness feel 'textured', 'rough', or 'oceanic' — no single pitch to latch onto. McAdams & Bregman (1979) J Acoust Soc Am; Leman et al. (2005) Musicae Scientiae
Vocal / Harmonic Content
RMS amplitude in 250 Hz – 2 kHz band 		The midrange band covers human voice and most melodic content. Vocal presence activates mirror-neuron and social-affective processing networks, modulating vagal tone. High mid-energy stages tend to feel interpersonally connected. Can you hear a voice, a melody, or a harmonic instrument? High = present and melodic; Low = bass-heavy drone or ambient wash with no tonal identity. Juslin & Västfjäll (2008) Behav Brain Sci; Trost et al. (2012) Neuropsychologia
