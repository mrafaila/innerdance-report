Morning Session — 27 June 2026¶

Conditions: Rest (sitting) · Work/Stress · Stage 4 music · Stage 10 music
Purpose: Test whether the fear-mechanism hypotheses from the full innerdance session (P1, Dec 2025) hold in a short controlled exposure using the same acoustic stimuli.

What this notebook does¶

A single 30-minute recording with four clearly defined phases:

Window Duration Condition
Rest (sitting) ~7 min Quiet sitting, no audio
Stress (work) ~10 min Cognitive work / mild stress, no audio
Stage 4 (music) ~4 min Life Sounds Stage 4 audio played
Stage 10 (music) ~5 min Life Sounds Stage 10 audio played

We compute HRV metrics using only functions from processing/hrv_core.py and acoustic features from the pre-computed Life Sounds dataset.
We then score how well H1 and H2 (from notebooks/hypothesis_fear_mechanism.ipynb) hold.

⚠️ N=1 — all findings are single-session observations. This notebook validates the hypothesis direction, not its statistical significance.

Metric validity reminder¶

Metric Valid condition Status
SD2 Any breathing rate ✅ Primary
SDNN Any breathing rate ✅ Primary
DFA α1 Breathing > 9 bpm ✅ When breathing rate confirmed (shown below)
RMSSD Pre/post resting only ⚠️ Unreliable when breathing < 9 bpm
SD1/SD2 Breathing confirmed ⚠️ Conditional — can be counterintuitive at rest

Key live demonstration in this dataset:
The Rest window has a measured breathing rate of ~9.4 bpm — just above the threshold.
RMSSD at Rest will appear lower than during Stress because slow RSA oscillations shift out of the RMSSD-sensitive HF band.
This is exactly the confound McCreary 2022 describes. SD2 and DFA α1 correctly show Rest as the highest-HRV window.

Step 1 — Load and preprocess the raw RR data¶

We use preprocess_rr() with use_mode_filter=True because this short recording has ectopic beats in the stress window (visible as 300–450 ms spikes in the raw data). The mode filter removes beats deviating >15% from the dominant histogram bin — a known approach for noisy short recordings (FIX-2 in hrv_core.py).

Critically, we pass actual nanosecond timestamps so that window slicing stays wall-clock aligned (not cumulative-RR drifted).

Step 6 — Hypothesis Likelihood Scoring¶

We score H1 and H2 using a metric-by-metric checklist. Each metric either supports, is neutral, or contradicts the hypothesis. A breathing-rate caveat is applied where relevant.

Expected directions:

  • H1 (Stage 4 is anxiety minimum): SD2 and SDNN minimum at Stage 4 or Stress; DFA α1 minimum at Stage 4 or Stress
  • H2 (Stage 10 is recovery): SD2 and DFA α1 higher at Stage 10 than Stage 4; partial return toward Rest baseline

Step 7 — What SD1/SD2 shows (and why to interpret it carefully)¶

SD1/SD2 shows an unexpected pattern: higher at Stage 4 than at Rest.
This seems counterintuitive — higher SD1/SD2 is classically associated with PNS dominance.

The explanation: during the Rest window, SDNN (slow oscillations) is very high relative to RMSSD.
This makes the Poincaré ellipse very elongated (lots of LF/slow variability), which produces a low SD1/SD2 ratio.
During Stage 4, total variability collapses (SDNN drops from 65→46 ms) while fast beat-to-beat variability (RMSSD) stays relatively higher — making the plot more circular, hence higher SD1/SD2.

The ratio reflects ellipse shape, not absolute autonomic state.
This confirms the recommendation: use SD2 and DFA α1 as primary session metrics. SD1/SD2 should not be interpreted in isolation.
