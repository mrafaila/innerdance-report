Sub-Bass Energy

sub_bass_energy 20 – 80 Hz RMS
CV across stages 0.386
01 · Description

Root-mean-square energy of the audio signal band-passed to 20–80 Hz. Below ~60 Hz, the cochlea's sensitivity drops sharply — this frequency range is primarily felt rather than heard, delivered to the body via the vestibular saccule, thoracic resonance, and bone conduction.

02 · How it sounds

A physical weight pressing against the sternum. Not a pitch you name — a sensation of being moved or held. At Stage 3 peak values, a sound engineer would describe it as 'room-filling low-end with no definable note — more like air pressure than audio.' Stage 1 feels like standing in a quiet room with a distant generator.

03 · Modular synthesis

Oscillator: dedicated sine-wave VCO tuned to 32–54 Hz (C1–A1). Sine is essential — harmonics push energy above the target band. Filter: 24 dB/oct LP VCF with cutoff at 90–100 Hz, resonance near zero. Amplitude: slow-attack VCA envelope (attack 4–8 s) to build gradually from Stage 3 onward. Recommended modules: Instruo Cs-L (sine output), Intellijel Morgasmatron (LP mode), Maths Ch.1/Ch.4 for slow rise. Route through a dedicated subwoofer send — main mix speakers rarely reproduce below 80 Hz.

Bass Energy

bass_energy 20 – 250 Hz RMS
CV across stages 0.337
01 · Description

Broadband low-frequency energy encompassing sub-bass through the upper bass range (kick transients, bass guitar fundamentals, low-string instruments). A superset of sub_bass_energy — it captures the full low-frequency weight of a track including harmonic content up to 250 Hz.

02 · How it sounds

The overall 'heaviness' or 'fullness' of the low end. Stage 3 would be described by a mastering engineer as 'bass-heavy, approaching a muddy low-mid build.' Stage 1 sits clean and open, with barely any low-end presence — an acoustic space rather than a bass statement.

03 · Modular synthesis

Same as sub-bass but with the LP VCF cutoff raised to 280–320 Hz to include upper-bass harmonics. Add distortion/saturation: Joranalogue Transmit 2 or WMD Aperture for harmonic enrichment in the 100–250 Hz zone. Dual-oscillator approach: one VCO at 32–54 Hz (fundamental), a second at an octave higher (65–108 Hz) mixed at -6 dB to fill out the band. Monitor with a spectrum analyser — ear fatigue in the low-mid range makes level judgement unreliable after 15 minutes.

HF Amplitude Modulation

hf_am_ratio AM envelope power at 0.15 – 0.40 Hz
CV across stages 0.643
01 · Description

Power of the Hilbert amplitude envelope in the high-frequency respiratory band (0.15–0.40 Hz = 9–24 breaths/min). When a track has high HF-AM energy, its volume is pulsing at roughly the pace of natural breathing. The pipeline hypothesis: this pacing entrains the listener's breathing rate, driving RSA and increasing HRV.

02 · How it sounds

A slow, rhythmic inhale-exhale swell in the overall volume — not a musical tremolo (which is usually faster), but a vast, breath-like tidal motion. Stage 12 (peak 0.41) would be described as 'the whole mix breathing.' Stage 8 (minimum 0.015) sounds comparatively static, even though it may have heavy rhythmic elements — the envelope does not oscillate at breathing frequency there.

03 · Modular synthesis

LFO rate: set to 0.15–0.40 Hz (period 2.5–6.7 s). Sine shape for smooth, biological feel. Target: route LFO → VCA CV input controlling master output level. This modulates the total mix amplitude, not any single element. Depth: attenuverter at 40–70% of LFO range to prevent silences between swells. Example patch: Maths Ch.1 (cycling, rate 0.2 Hz) → VCA (Veils or Blinds) → master out. Warning: at Stage 8 in Life Sounds, HF-AM is intentionally suppressed to prevent breathing entrainment during the activation peak.

↗ Open source

★★★★★ Nature Publishing Group (Communications Biology). Multi-experiment study (fMRI + EEG). This is Brain.fm's disclosed mechanism paper. Highest trust in the acoustic feature set.

LF Amplitude Modulation

lf_am_ratio AM envelope power at 0.04 – 0.15 Hz
CV across stages 0.717
01 · Description

Power of the Hilbert amplitude envelope in the low-frequency band (0.04–0.15 Hz = 2.4–9 breaths/min). When elevated, the track is swelling at very slow rates — slower than normal breathing. This matters critically for HRV measurement validity: if LF-AM paces breathing below 9 bpm, RMSSD and DFA α1 become unreliable.

02 · How it sounds

An almost tidal, geological swell — so slow it barely registers as rhythm. Stage 9 (max 0.38) feels like the music itself is breathing one long inhale over 8–10 seconds and exhaling just as slowly. Stage 8 (min 0.046) has no such macro-movement; it feels dense and static in amplitude.

03 · Modular synthesis

LFO rate: 0.04–0.12 Hz (period 8–25 s). Most Eurorack LFOs have a minimum rate that doesn't reach this range — use Maths in cycle mode with a long time constant (right slope 8–20 s), or a dedicated slow LFO (Batumi, Zadar). Warning for facilitators: extended LF-AM at full depth during Stages 4 and 9 (highest LF-AM values in Life Sounds) risks entraining breathing below 9 bpm, making HRV metrics unreliable at those stages. Consider monitoring breathing rate directly if HRV interpretation is critical.

AM Depth

am_depth Hilbert envelope coefficient of variation
CV across stages 0.511
01 · Description

The coefficient of variation of the smoothed Hilbert amplitude envelope — a frequency-agnostic measure of how dynamically the overall volume fluctuates. High AM depth = large, slow swells between near-silence and full intensity. Low AM depth = consistent, steady-state volume. Unlike hf_am_ratio and lf_am_ratio, this captures total modulation depth regardless of its rate.

02 · How it sounds

Stage 5 (max 1.01, where CV ≥ 1 means the standard deviation exceeds the mean) would be described as 'vast, shapeless tidal dynamics — the music collapses into near-silence and surges back.' Stage 7 (min 0.21) feels comparatively 'produced and controlled' — consistent level with deliberate dynamic management.

03 · Modular synthesis

LFO depth control: the attenuverter (or VCA) on the LFO output that feeds the master VCA is the primary dial. High depth: full unattenuated LFO range (0 to +5V) → VCA CV. Low depth: attenuated to 10–20% range → subtle tremolo only. Shape matters: sine/triangle = smooth; random/S&H = unpredictable collapses. Stage 5 (Disintegration) likely uses random or stepped shapes. Module suggestion: Instruo ochd (8-channel LFO, all running at different rates) summed and normalised gives naturalistic high-depth modulation without an identifiable period.

04 · Literature reference

Lehrer PM & Gevirtz R (2014). Heart rate variability biofeedback: how and why does it work? Frontiers in Psychology 5:756.

↗ Open source
"Amplitude of oscillations in heart rate at the resonance frequency of the baroreflex feedback loop (approximately 0.1 Hz, 6 breaths/min) are maximised by paced breathing at this frequency, producing the largest possible HRV response — the resonance effect."
★★★★★ Frontiers in Psychology, peer-reviewed meta-analysis. Lehrer is the leading HRV-B researcher; this paper is widely cited (3,000+).
INDIRECT — establishes that AM depth at the right frequency produces maximal HRV response. AM depth without rate specificity is a broader, less targeted measure. The physiological claim extends from rate-specific (HF/LF AM) to depth-general (this feature).

Spectral Centroid

spec_centroid_hz Centre of mass of the spectrum (Hz)
CV across stages 0.263
01 · Description

The frequency-weighted mean of the power spectrum — the 'brightness' of a sound. A spectral centroid of 419 Hz (Stage 1) feels warm and bass-dominated; 1013 Hz (Stage 9) feels bright and mid-forward. Implemented via librosa feature.spectral_centroid, averaged across 30-second windows.

02 · How it sounds

Stage 9 (Strength-Failure, 1013 Hz) would be described as 'cutting — the mix sits in your ears rather than your body. There's presence and edge.' Stage 1 (Safety, 419 Hz): 'warm, rounded, almost like listening through heavy fabric — no harsh edges anywhere.' A mix engineer would reach for a high-shelf EQ cut to move from Stage 9 toward Stage 1.

03 · Modular synthesis

Primary control: VCF cutoff frequency. Low cutoff (400–600 Hz) + steep roll-off = low centroid. Open cutoff (2–5 kHz) = higher centroid. To add brightness without losing body: parallel filter topology — Intellijel Morgasmatron in parallel mode, one LP and one HP summed. Increase the HP mix ratio to raise the centroid without removing bass. Automated arc: slow envelope or LFO → VCF CV, rising through the session arc (Stages 1→9) then falling back (Stages 9→12). In Life Sounds, Stage 11 (854 Hz) shows a partial brightness recovery before the final reintegration.

Onset Strength

onset_strength librosa onset envelope mean
CV across stages 0.119
01 · Description

Mean of the onset strength envelope — how much rhythmic 'punch' the track delivers per unit time. High values indicate clear, frequent transients (kicks, snares, plucks). Low values indicate ambient, sustained textures without strong rhythmic events. Computed via librosa onset.onset_strength.

02 · How it sounds

Stage 8 (Choice, max 1.065): 'Driving, assertive — every beat lands with authority. You feel it in the sternum.' Stage 1 (Safety, min 0.695): 'Textural, no pulse you could tap to. The music arrives like weather, not a clock.' Onset strength is the feature a DJ reads as 'energy' when deciding whether a track will fill a floor.

03 · Modular synthesis

Fast-attack envelopes: attack 1–5 ms, decay 50–200 ms. Each gate trigger from a clock/sequencer fires one onset event. Higher density = higher onset strength: subdivided clock (÷2, ÷4 triggers) increases onset rate. Transient design: give each onset a percussive accent — brief pitch envelope on a VCO (Plaits in percussion mode), or noise burst through HP filter (Mutable Instruments Rings in excite mode). For Stage 1 texture: suppress all gate triggers; let drones and slow modulation carry the mix.

04 · Literature reference

↗ Open source

Passive listening does not trigger this ANS phase-locking." Large & Jones 1999: "Strong onset events act as phase-setting stimuli that reset internal attentional oscillations, entraining neural attending pulses to the beat of external rhythmic events."

Onset Irregularity

onset_cv CV of onset strength envelope
CV across stages 0.339
01 · Description

Coefficient of variation of the onset strength signal — how irregular the rhythm is. Low onset_cv (Stages 9–12) means a steady, predictable pulse. High onset_cv (Stages 2–3) means the rhythmic energy arrives in unpredictable bursts. This feature operationalises rhythmic surprise.

02 · How it sounds

Stage 3 (Threshold, max 0.855): 'Polyrhythmic or loosely structured — you keep expecting the beat to land and it doesn't, or it lands somewhere you didn't anticipate.' Stage 10 (Dark-Night, min 0.312): 'You could set a metronome to this. The rhythm is a container, not a disruption.'

03 · Modular synthesis

Clock jitter: inject a random voltage (Turing Machine, Marbles T output) into the clock CV input. More randomness = higher onset_cv. Probabilistic gates: Pamela's New Workout with 'probability' setting, or Mutable Instruments Branches. Euclidean rhythms at odd ratios: 7 hits over 12 steps creates a non-repeating feel. For Stage 10–12 regularity: clean 4/4 clock, no randomisation, minimal variation. The shift from high onset_cv in Stages 2–3 to low in Stages 9–12 mirrors the innerdance arc from disruption to containment.

Zero-Crossing Rate

zcr librosa ZCR mean
CV across stages 0.298
01 · Description

Rate at which the audio waveform crosses zero amplitude per unit time. High ZCR = noisy, percussive, or high-frequency-dominant content. Low ZCR = tonal, sustained, low-frequency dominant. Acts as a fast proxy for timbral character — a Stradivarius and white noise have very different ZCR profiles.

02 · How it sounds

Stage 6 (Awakening, max 0.050): 'There's air in this mix — cymbals, reverb tails, texture. It feels like the sound is moving around the room.' Stage 12 (Reintegration, min 0.018): 'Pure, fundamental tones. Sine waves and soft pads. No harsh edges anywhere. The silence between notes is audible.'

03 · Modular synthesis

Noise/oscillator mix ratio: a crossfader (Intellijel Crossfold, or simple VCA) between a VCO sine/saw output and a white noise source controls ZCR continuously. Full oscillator side = low ZCR. Full noise side = high ZCR. High-frequency content: raising VCF cutoff above 3 kHz dramatically increases ZCR even with tonal sources. For Stage 6 effect: add a dedicated hi-hat voice (FM drum or noise + VCA with short decay) in addition to the main mix.

04 · Literature reference

↗ Open source

Spectral Flatness

spectral_flatness Wiener entropy (0 = tonal, 1 = noise)
CV across stages 1.378
01 · Description

Ratio of geometric mean to arithmetic mean of the power spectrum (Wiener entropy). Values near 0 indicate a very tonal signal (energy concentrated in a few frequencies). Values near 1 indicate white-noise-like spectral distribution. Stage 1 (0.000019) is among the most tonal sounds measurable — essentially a sine wave with barely any harmonic content. Stage 9 (0.00169) is 90× more noise-like.

02 · How it sounds

Stage 1 (Safety, near 0): 'Glassy, pure — like a singing bowl or a single cello harmonic held in a reverb chamber. No grit, no noise floor, almost unsettling in its purity.' Stage 9 (Strength-Failure, max): 'Tonally complex — there's something deliberate happening in every octave, not just chaos, but the spectrum is actively occupied.' Even at max, Life Sounds values are still very low — this is an extraordinarily tonal playlist.

03 · Modular synthesis

Extremely low flatness (near Stage 1): single VCO in sine mode, no filter (or LP filter at very high Q with minimal drive), no noise, in a long reverb with pre-delay. Any additional oscillator, saturation, or FM immediately raises spectral flatness significantly. Raising flatness toward Stage 9: introduce FM (Plaits FM index), wavefolder (Instruo Cs-L folder), or ring modulation to add inharmonic content. Note: even Stage 9 remains far below 0.01 — this playlist is exceptionally tonal throughout.

Spectral Flux

spectral_flux L2 norm of consecutive STFT column differences
CV across stages 0.383
01 · Description

Rate of change in the spectral content between successive short-time frames — how rapidly the timbral texture evolves. High spectral flux = rapid textural mutation (Stage 3, 42.6). Low spectral flux = stable, static timbre (Stage 1, 9.1). Computed as the mean L2 norm of successive STFT frame differences.

02 · How it sounds

Stage 3 (Threshold, max): 'The texture keeps shifting — you can't quite hold onto what the sound is doing. It's never the same twice in any two-second window.' This is the most acoustically 'unsettled' stage in the set. Stage 1 (Safety, min): 'Completely stable — you could analyse one second of this and describe the whole track. Nothing moves that doesn't need to.'

03 · Modular synthesis

High flux: many simultaneously modulated parameters. Wavefolder depth, VCF cutoff, FM index, and reverb time all changing on different LFO rates (Maths + ochd + Marbles) with no synchronisation creates constant spectral movement. Cross-modulation: VCO1 FM → VCO2, VCO2 output → Rings → Clouds: each stage changes the spectrum unpredictably. Low flux (Stage 1): all modulators at minimum rate or switched off. Single static filter setting, no FM, reverb frozen.

04 · Literature reference

No direct HRV citation. Spectral flux is an MIR descriptor used in music structure segmentation (Foote & Cooper 2001; Scheirer 1998).

No external source

Tempo Change Rate

tempo_change_rate CV of 4-second windowed BPM estimates
CV across stages 0.455
01 · Description

Coefficient of variation of tempo estimates computed over successive 4-second windows — how unstable or variable the perceived BPM is. A high value means the music is accelerating, decelerating, or irregularly pulsing. A low value means the tempo is steady and predictable. Added to the feature set June 2026 based on expert feedback: sudden tempo shifts at Stage 6→7→8 were identified as a distinct activation mechanism.

02 · How it sounds

Stage 2 (Dissatisfaction, max 0.167): 'It keeps pulling at you — you lean into the beat and it shifts. Intentionally unsettling. A DJ would call this 'train-wreck energy' if it were accidental; here it reads as deliberate destabilisation.' Stage 12 (Reintegration, min 0.037): 'You could programme a drum machine to this. The tempo is a fixed container — the music is doing everything else, but the pulse never wavers.'

03 · Modular synthesis

Variable clock: inject slow random CV (Marbles X output at low probability) into a clock module's rate input (Pamela's New Workout: external CV on BPM parameter). Small amounts of random offset = subtle humanisation. Large random CV = Stage 2 instability. Envelope-controlled clock: an envelope that speeds up and slows down the master clock in response to the session arc creates deliberate structural tempo shifts (Stages 6→8 acceleration). Reticular / vestibular mechanism: sudden tempo shifts trigger an orienting response via the reticular activating system independently of sub-bass SPL — validated by session expert during June 2026 review.

04 · Literature reference

Watanabe K, Ooishi Y & Kashino M (2017). Heart rate responses induced by acoustic tempo and its interaction with basal heart rate. Scientific Reports 7:43856. doi:10.1038/srep43856. Supporting: Bretherton B, Deuchars J & Windsor WL (2019). Effects of exposure to musical beats on heart rate variability. Music & Science 2:1–16. doi:10.1177/2059204319858281.
